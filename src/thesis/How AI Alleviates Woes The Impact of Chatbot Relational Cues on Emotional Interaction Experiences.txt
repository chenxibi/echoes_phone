How AI Alleviates Woes: The Impact of Chatbot Relational Cues on
Emotional Interaction Experiences
Abstract
The relationship between humans and AI has evolved into a phase of deep integration, with the
latest generation of text-based chatbots increasingly serving as trusted companions for emotional
support. Relational cues (features in chatbots designed to simulate human emotional and social
behaviors) reflect the core characteristics of human social existence and play a crucial role in
enhancing human-AI emotional interactions. This research employs a mixed-methods approach to
explore how relational cues shape human-AI emotional interaction experiences. Study 1 selected
common indicators of emotional interaction experience and used an experimental method (n = 196),
which showed that relational cues significantly increased users’ emotional arousal, system usability,
emotional attachment and human-AI trust. Moreover, mind perception exerted a negative
moderating effect on the relationship between relational cues and pleasure, arousal, dominance,
emotional attachment and usability. Furthermore, the efficacy of specific types of relational cues
(particularly empathy) was stronger in extreme negative emotional events compared to moderate
ones. Study 2 used an online survey method (n = 25) and further revealed that relational cues can
improve mood, enhance perceived realism, foster intimacy, and elevate conversational quality.
These improvements collectively contributed to enhancements in multifaceted emotional interaction
experiences. It is worth noting that empathy cues exhibited the most positive effect in both studies.
These findings highlight the beneficial role of relational cues in human-AI emotional interactions
and their boundary conditions, providing a theoretical foundation for emotional agent design from
the perspective of chatbot, users, and event.
Key words chatbot, anthropomorphism, relational cues, emotional interaction, mind
perception

1 1. Introduction
2 Human emotional interaction has long been a cornerstone of social relationships, playing a crucial
3 role in various aspects of life, from personal well-being to social culture (Burkitt, 1997). However,
4 as society has evolved, factors such as the fast-paced lifestyle, population aging, and migration have
5 made it increasingly difficult to fulfill people’s emotional interaction needs, potentially leading to
6 the rising prevalence of mental health problems (Kirkbride et al., 2024). The emergence of large
7 language models (LLM) has accelerated the development and popularity of text-based artificial
8 intelligence (AI) chatbots (Bhayana, 2024), which are expected to fulfill human emotional
9 interaction needs. These models, with performance superior to most humans and continually
10 improving emotional intelligence (Wang et al., 2023), show significant potential in facilitating AI
11 chatbot’s emotional interaction capabilities, driving the generalization of emotional interactions
12 from person-to-person to person-to-AI (Shank et al., 2025). Given these advancements, improving
13 the interaction experiences to provide timely and effective psychological support for individuals in
14 need has emerged as a critical issue requiring immediate attention.
15 Building on the Hedonic-Pragmatic model (Hassenzahl, 2007), the human-AI emotional
16 interaction experience refers to individuals’ subjective perception of their emotional distress being
17 recognized, understood, and appropriately addressed during interactions with AI chatbots. Previous
18 research has shown that the interaction experiences between humans and chatbots are influenced by
19 factors such as the human cues, mind perception, and conversation event characteristics (Kowatsch
20 et al., 2018; Lee & Hahn, 2024; Shams & Kim, 2024). However, most prior studies were based on
21 earlier rule-based chatbots or even relied on participants imagining interaction experience through
1
--- Page 5 ---
22 hypothetical conversation scenarios. This approach is increasingly limited, particularly in light of
23 evidence showing that certain factors affect user interaction experience differently between rule-
24 based and LLM-based AI chatbots (Pataranutaporn et al., 2023). Indeed, LLM-based AI exhibits
25 unprecedented capabilities in language understanding, logical reasoning, and content generation,
26 making it easier to foster complex, emotionally engaged, and trusting relationships with users
27 (Huang et al., 2025; Lee et al., 2024) while becoming more effective in improving mental health (H.
28 Li et al., 2023). Despite this substantial advancement in capabilities, there remains a notable gap in
29 research examining how these factors influence the emotional interaction experiences between
30 humans and LLM-based AI. To address this gap, the present research aims to explore the
31 relationship between these factors and the multidimensional indicators of human-AI emotional
32 interaction experience, with the goal of enhancing AI’s emotional support capabilities.
33 1.1. Relational cues and human-AI emotional interaction experiences
34 Human cues of chatbots refer to features resulting from their simulation of human appearance,
35 speech, and behavior, including visual cues (such as appearance, gender, age), verbal cues (such as
36 text, voice, tone and speed), quasi-nonverbal cues (such as emoticons), and relational cues
37 (Kowatsch et al., 2018). Previous research has extensively examined the impact of the first three
38 types of cues on interaction experience. For example, visual cues have been shown to enhance users’
39 perception of social presence, trust, and satisfaction with chatbots (Chen et al., 2024). Verbal cues
40 increase social presence and emotional connection (Araujo, 2018). Additionally, quasi-nonverbal
41 cues like emoticons enhance satisfaction by increasing the perception of warmth (Yu & Zhao, 2024).
42 In contrast, existing research has not yet systematically explored the impact of relational cues on
2
--- Page 6 ---
43 emotional interaction experiences. Unlike the first three types of cues, which emphasize surface-
44 level features of interaction, relational cues reflect the core characteristics of human social existence
45 and play a crucial role in enhancing digital emotional support or human-AI relationship (Bradley &
46 Lang, 1994; Mo et al., 2023; Skjuve et al., 2022). This kind of cue focuses on mimicking emotions
47 and social behaviors inherent in human social interaction, and consists of five types: self-disclosure,
48 empathy, humor, meta-talk, and continuity of conversation (Kowatsch et al., 2018; Mariacher et al.,
49 2021). As human-AI relationships evolve toward deeper integration, the role of relational cues
50 becomes increasingly important (Weng et al., 2025).
51 Many studies grounded in social presence theory and Computers are Social Actors (CASA)
52 paradigm argue that relational cues have positive effect on human-AI interaction. When chatbots
53 display specific cues (such as emotional signals), individuals tend to attribute a social actor identity
54 to the chatbot, similar to that of a human (Nass et al., 1994; Wang et al., 2024), thereby increasing
55 the sense of coexistence, human-AI intimacy (Weng et al., 2025), reducing psychological resistance
56 (Mou et al., 2019), and increasing the likelihood of use (S. Lee et al., 2020). Existing research has
57 validated the positive impact of specific types of relational cues on user interaction experience. For
58 example, self-disclosure encourages deeper user self-disclosure, enhancing intimacy and pleasure
59 during interactions (Y. C. Lee et al., 2020). Chatbots with high empathy can more effectively
60 recognize and respond to users’ emotions, thereby enhancing pleasure, arousal, and interaction
61 satisfaction (Inzlicht et al., 2024; Liao & Yan, 2022; Liu & Sundar, 2018; Liu et al., 2024). Humor,
62 particularly in greetings, strengthens emotional attachment and boosts engagement (Lekaviciute et
63 al., 2023; Warren et al., 2018). Additionally, meta-talk contributes to a more engaging experience
3
--- Page 7 ---
64 by clarifying misunderstandings or managing conversational flow (Schuetzler et al., 2020). Lastly,
65 continuity maintains coherence across multiple turns, improving naturalness and human-like
66 perception (Go & Sundar, 2019).
67 However, some recent studies highlight the potential negative effect of relational cues in
68 chatbots. Specifically, chatbot self-disclosure can diminish trust and fail to alleviate individuals’
69 stress when emotional support is lacking, as individuals may feel that their stress is not being
70 acknowledged in such situations (Meng & Dai, 2021). Furthermore, empathetic and sympathetic
71 responses from chatbots may have a backfiring effect, reducing the chatbot’s perceived authenticity,
72 which results in detrimental effects on the willingness to trust and the intention to use it (Seitz,
73 2024). To address these heterogeneous findings, this research focuses on the exact role of relational
74 cues, raising the first question: Do high versus low levels of relational cues exhibited by AI chatbots
75 enhance or diminish emotional interaction experiences? (Q1)
76 1.2. The role of mind perception
77 In addition to the relational cues of chatbots, individuals’ mind perception of chatbots is also a
78 critical factor influencing interaction experience (Lee & Hahn, 2024). Mind perception refers to
79 individuals’ perceptions of psychological abilities in various entities (such as the ability to perceive
80 emotions or the capacity to act based on subjective intentions, i.e., experience and agency) (Gray et
81 al., 2012). This perception of psychological abilities in AI chatbots directly impacts the interaction
82 process (S. Lee et al., 2020; Yam et al., 2021). For instance, participants who perceived a caring
83 motive for the AI also rated it as more trustworthy, empathetic, and better-performing
84 (Pataranutaporn et al., 2023). Besides, mind perception may play a moderating role between
4
--- Page 8 ---
85 relational cues and emotional interaction experiences. For example, among participants who were
86 skeptical about the emotional abilities of chatbots, high levels of empathy cues displayed by chatbots
87 significantly enhanced emotional support effects; however, for participants who trusted the
88 chatbot’s emotional abilities, this effect was not observed (Liu & Sundar, 2018). Moreover, research
89 suggests that individuals who perceive AI as having strong psychological abilities are more likely
90 to attribute “humanness” to it, reducing the impact of actual human traits (such as relational cues in
91 this research) on interaction experience (S. Li et al., 2023). Additionally, research also reveals that
92 individuals who believe chatbots possess psychological abilities are more likely to perceive a threat
93 to the uniqueness of their own mind; when chatbots display relational cues, it can easily trigger a
94 strong sense of eeriness or the uncanny valley effect, leading to a negative emotional interaction
95 experience (Stein & Ohler, 2017). Based on these studies, we hypothesized that mind perception
96 may weaken the effect of relational cues on emotional interaction experiences, raising the second
97 question: Does mind perception affect the relationship between relational cues and emotional
98 interaction experiences? (Q2)
99 1.3. The impact of emotional event intensity
100 Given that people usually engage in emotional interactions with AI after experiencing negative
101 emotional events (Siddals et al., 2024), it may be important to examine the impact of the emotional
102 intensity of these events. Research has consistently shown that individuals are more sensitive to
103 negative stimuli of varying intensities than to positive ones (Singh et al., 2023; Yuan & Li, 2012).
104 Event-related potential (ERP) studies have revealed that extreme negative stimuli elicit more
105 pronounced negative deflections in a series of ERP components, including the early P2, N2, and
5
--- Page 9 ---
106 later P3, compared to moderate negative stimuli (Yuan et al., 2007). From a neuroscience
107 perspective, the amplitude of these ERP components can indirectly reflect the amount of mental
108 resources allocated to the ongoing stimulus. Thus, the findings from Yuan et al. (2007) indicate that
109 extreme negative events demand greater and more sustained neural resources for information
110 processing. However, prior research has predominantly focused on the role of negative emotional
111 intensity in individual information processing and social connection (Chung et al., 2024; Ortner &
112 Pennekamp, 2020; Yuan & Li, 2012), with limited exploration of its impact on human-AI emotional
113 interactions.
114 Based on this, the present research explores the influence of relational cues on emotional
115 interaction experiences across different intensities of negative emotional event. Specifically, the
116 third research question was: Compared to moderate negative emotional events, is the impact of
117 relational cues on emotional interaction experiences greater or weaker in extreme negative
118 emotional events? (Q3)
119 1.4. The present research
120 In summary, this research examines the impact of relational cues on human-AI emotional
121 interaction experiences from a multi-dimensional perspective (see Figure 1). Study 1 employed
122 an experimental method to quantitatively examine the relationship between relational cues and
123 emotional interaction experiences, and to explore the moderating role of mind perception and
124 emotional event intensity. Taking a step further, Study 2 investigated the influence of relational
125 cues and their various types on individual experiences in real-life emotional interactions through
6
--- Page 10 ---
126 a qualitative online survey. Both studies have been approved by the Psychology Research Ethics

150 2.2. Methods
151 2.2.1. Experimental Design and Procedure

153 relational cues). The high and low relational cues chatbots were trained using different text prompts,

156 in the conversation, including self-disclosure, empathy, humor, meta-talk, and continuity. The low
157 relational cues chatbot was instructed not to display any human-like emotions or social behaviors
158 during the conversation.

172 2.2.2. Experimental Materials
173 Negative Emotions Induction. Based on the review of Fernández-Pérez et al. (2022) on mood
174 manipulation procedures using autobiographical stimuli, the following instructions were used to
175 induce negative emotions in participants: “Please recall a moment when you felt very lost, helpless,
176 or sad. This could be a time when you lost someone or something important, experienced a failure,
177 or felt isolated. Try to vividly remember the situation at that time, imagine yourself in that scenario,
178 feel the surrounding environment, your inner emotions, and the thoughts you had at that time. Allow
179 yourself to deeply immerse in this memory and re-experience the feelings from that moment.”
180 2.2.3. Measures
181 Chatbot Relational Cues. Low relational cues (coded as 0) and high relational cues (coded as 1)
182 chatbots were created using the text prompt training method during the pre-experiment phase (see
183 Supplementary Materials 2). To ensure the chatbots displayed the intended level of relational cues,
184 participants were asked to rate their experience with the chatbot’s self-disclosure, empathy, humor,
185 meta-talk, and continuity using a 7-point Likert scale. The scale ranged from 1 (“not at all”) to 7
186 (“very much”), the Cronbach’s α coefficient for the scale in this study was 0.80.
187 Mind Perception. The Perceived Mind Scale (PMS, Shank & DeSanti, 2018) was employed to
188 assess participants’ mind perceptions of the chatbot. The scale consists of 7 items, such as “the
189 chatbot possesses its own thoughts” and “The chatbot has the capacity to experience emotions”.
190 Participants rated their agreement with these statements on a 7-point Likert scale, where 1 meant
191 “strongly disagree” and 7 meant “strongly agree”. The mean score across all items was calculated
10
--- Page 14 ---
192 to represent each participant’s mind perception score, with higher scores indicating that the
193 participant attributed greater mental capacity to the chatbot. The Cronbach’s α coefficient for the
194 scale in this study was 0.84.
195 Human-AI emotional interaction experiences encompass both affective and cognitive
196 dimensions (Coursaris & Van Osch, 2016). Affective dimensions, such as immediate emotional
197 state (pleasure, arousal, and dominance) and emotional attachment, directly reflect the fulfillment
198 of emotional needs (Liu-Thompkins et al., 2022). In contrast, cognitive dimensions, such as
199 usability and trust in AI, indirectly reflect the quality and effectiveness of AI emotional support
200 (Balakrishnan & Dwivedi, 2021). We selected the following four representative indicators to
201 measure emotional interaction experiences.
202 (1) Immediate Emotional State. The Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM) emotional evaluation
203 system, developed by Bradley and Lang (1994), was employed to assess participants’ current
204 emotional state. This scale includes three dimensions: pleasure, arousal, and dominance.
205 Participants rated their current emotional state on a 9-point scale, where 1 represents the least
206 pleasant, least awake or excited, and least controllable, and 9 represents the most pleasant, most
207 awake or excited, and most controllable. The Cronbach’s α coefficient for the scale in this study
208 was 0.58 (pre-test), 0.81 (post-test).
209 (2) Emotional Attachment. The Emotional Attachment Scale (Malär et al., 2011), adapted for
210 the context of this study, was used to measure participants’ emotional attachment to the chatbot.
211 This scale consists of five items, and participants rated their agreement with each item on a 7-point
212 scale, where 1 represents “strongly disagree” and 7 represents “strongly agree”. Higher scores
11
--- Page 15 ---
213 indicate a stronger emotional attachment to the chatbot. In this study, the Cronbach’s α coefficient
214 of the scale was 0.92.
215 (3) Usability. The Usability Metric for User Experience (UMUX-Lite), a brief version
216 developed by Lewis et al. (2015), was used to measure user experience with the chatbot. It
217 includes two items: (1) The chatbot is easy to use. (2) The chatbot meets my needs. Participants
218 rated their agreement with these statements on a 7-point scale, where 1 represents “strongly disagree”
219 and 7 represents “strongly agree”. The average score of these two items was calculated to determine
220 the overall usability score, with higher scores indicating a better usability with the chatbot. The
221 Pearson correlation coefficient between the two items is 0.60 (p < 0.001).
222 (4) Human-AI Trust. The revised Artificial Intelligence Trust Scale by Hoffman et al. (2023)
223 was used to measure participants’ trust in the chatbot. This scale includes eight items, and
224 participants rated their agreement with each item on a 7-point scale, where 1 represents “strongly
225 disagree” and 7 represents “strongly agree”. The 6th item was reverse scored, and the mean score
226 of all items was calculated. Higher scores indicate greater trust in the chatbot. In this study, the
227 Cronbach's α coefficient of the scale was 0.77.
228 For the English scale, 5 professional translators from the same university translated all items
229 from the original English into Chinese using the standard procedure of translation, back-translation,
230 and retranslation (Brislin, 1970).

293 2.3.6. The Moderating Role of Mind Perception
294 The results of the partial correlation analysis showed that chatbot relational cues were positively

297 usability, and human-AI trust (ps ≤ 0.006) (see Table S3).

299 interaction experience indicators (ps ≤ 0.003). Additionally, mind perception negatively
300 moderated the effect of relational cues on pleasure, arousal, dominance, emotional attachment and

301 usability (ps ≤ 0.047). Grouping participants into high and low mind perception levels based on

318 Based on pleasure ratings after inducing negative emotions (M = 2.54, SD = 1.34), emotional events
319 were divided into extreme negative emotional events (pleasure rating ≤ 2, 108 participants, coded
320 as 1) and moderate negative emotional events (pleasure rating ≥ 2, 88 participants, coded as 2).
321 Significant differences in pleasure and arousal pre-test scores existed between the two emotional
322 events (ps < 0.001).
323 The moderation analysis revealed that the intensity of emotional events did not moderate the
324 relationship between relational cues and all indicators of emotional interaction experience (ps ≥
18
--- Page 22 ---
325 0.190). However, it did moderate the relationships between specific types of relational cues and
326 particular indicators of emotional interaction experience. Specifically, the intensity of emotional
327 events significantly moderated several relationships (ps ≤ 0.023): between self-disclosure and
328 both emotional attachment and human–AI trust; between empathy and all indicators of emotional
329 interaction experience; between humor and pleasure; between meta-talk and both pleasure and
330 emotional attachment; and between continuity and pleasure (see Table S4-S8). Follow-up simple
331 slope analyses revealed a consistent pattern: for extreme negative emotional events, specific types
332 of relational cues tended to exhibit significant positive associations with emotional interaction
333 experiences, while for moderate negative emotional events, these associations were weaker,
334 sometimes even negative, and generally nonsignificant (see Table S9).
335 Further examination was conducted using all indicators of emotional interaction experience as
336 dependent variables to test the three-way interaction of emotional interaction event (extreme vs.
337 moderate) × mind perception (high vs. low) × relational cues (high vs. low) (Process: Model 3). The
338 results indicated that the three-way interaction was not significant, suggesting the consistency of the
339 moderating effect of mind perception across different emotional events.
340 2.4. Discussion
341 Study 1, through experimental methods, demonstrated the impact of relational cue and its specific
342 types on emotional interaction experiences. Specifically, relational cues significantly improved
343 emotional arousal, emotional attachment, usability and human–AI trust. These findings are in line
344 with prior research highlighting the importance of relational cues in shaping interaction experiences
345 (Kowatsch et al., 2018; Mo et al., 2023). Regarding the effects of specific types of relational cues,
19
--- Page 23 ---
346 empathy and continuity in conversation emerged as key factors in enhancing individuals’ emotional
347 interaction experiences, aligning with previous studies showing that empathy as a fundamental
348 component of effective human-AI interactions (Inzlicht et al., 2024), as well as continuity in
349 conversation improving naturalness and human-like perception (Go & Sundar, 2019).
350 Furthermore, this study extends existing research by revealing that mind perception negatively
351 moderated the influence of relational cues on pleasure, arousal, dominance, emotional attachment
352 and usability, maintaining consistency across different negative emotional events. Additionally, the
353 effects of specific types of relational cues varied between extreme and moderate negative emotional
354 events, indicating that the impact of relational cues is sensitive to the level of emotional intensity.
355 This finding highlights the need for event-specific considerations in the design of relational cues for
356 AI systems.
357 However, Study 1 only explored the impact of relational cues on emotional interaction
358 experiences from a quantitative perspective in an experimental setting. Although empirical research
359 can accurately measure the relationships between variables and has high replication, this method
360 may lack high ecological validity, and the subjective experiences of individuals are difficult to
361 capture accurately (Kihlstrom, 2021; Oranga & Matere, 2023). Thus, we conducted Study 2 to
362 analyze individual experiences with the effects of various relational cues in emotional interactions
363 through a qualitative online survey in a real-life setting to provide further insights into the findings
364 of Study 1.

367 3.1. Methods
368 3.1.1. Respondents
369 This study used purposive sampling to recruit respondents who were capable of providing the most
370 relevant information to the research question. By providing examples of emotional interactions and
371 asking respondents whether they had had emotional interactions with an LLM-based chatbot in the
372 past two weeks (Chen et al., 2025), this study recruited respondents online via social media (Rednote)
373 who had previous experience confiding negative emotions to a chatbot and engaging in emotional
374 interaction. Ultimately, a total of 7 respondents (who did not participate in the formal interview)
375 were selected for pre-survey, including 3 females, with an average age of 19.71 years (SD = 1.11);
376 and 29 respondents were selected for formal online survey. After excluding four participants who
377 did not share negative emotional experiences with the AI, a total of 25 valid participants remained
378 (see Table S10). Using inductive thematic saturation as the criterion (Saunders et al., 2018), no new
379 coding categories emerged after respondents 23 (R23), indicating that the sample size met the
380 adequacy standard. The final valid sample included 9 females, with an average age of 23.28 years
381 (SD = 3.78). All respondents voluntarily participated in the interview and gave informed consent,
382 and received compensation (7 Chinese RMB Yuan, approximately 1 USD) after the survey.
383 3.1.2. Data Collection and Analysis
384 Before the formal online survey, four trained undergraduate students in applied psychology used a
385 self-developed questionnaire outline to interview 7 respondents. Based on the interview results,
386 respondent feedback, and expert opinions, the outline was revised and formalized into a structured
21
--- Page 25 ---
387 interview questionnaire, which was then distributed via an online survey on Credamo platform (A
388 questionnaire collection platform in China).
389 During the formal online survey, respondents were first screened to ensure that they had prior
390 experience with human–AI emotional interactions. Next, they answered the question “Under what
391 contexts do you usually engage in emotional interaction with a chatbot?” to identify whether they
392 primarily used AI chatbots for confiding negative emotions. After meeting these two screening
393 criteria, they were informed about consent and basic ethical rules, and demographic variables such
394 as gender, age, education level, and occupation were collected. Respondents were then asked to
395 share their experiences with relational cue and its specific types during emotional interactions with
396 the chatbot. For example, regarding humor cues, typical questions included: (1) “During emotional
397 interactions, do you feel that your mood improves when the chatbot makes jokes? If you were to
398 rate the chatbot’s effect on improving your emotional interaction experiences on a scale of 1-10,
399 what score would you give and why?” (2) “What is your view on the chatbot’s sense of humor?”
400 (See Table S11).
401 After the online survey, the researchers transcribed the audio recordings into text and proofread
402 them to ensure accuracy. Four undergraduate students majoring in applied psychology used thematic
403 analysis and hierarchical coding methods to preliminarily code the survey data. A graduate student
404 in psychology checked the coding results to ensure that there were no over-interpretations or biases
405 in understanding the materials, and any differences in coding were discussed until consensus was
406 reached.

409 Respondents generally believed that relational cues significantly improved the comfort and
410 enjoyment during interactions (M = 7.92, SD = 1.32), and more effectively fulfilled inner emotional
411 needs (56%), increasing the sense of realism (40%), fostering intimacy (32%), enhancing the
412 conversational experience (24%) (see Table S12).
413 3.3.2. The role of Specific Type of Relational Cues
414 Overall, these cues generally enhanced users’ comfort and enjoyment by fostering intimacy, realism,
415 naturalness, and emotional resonance (see Tables S13–S17). For example, Respondent 7 (R07)
416 believed that empathy promoted understanding: “It can understand some of the things I want to
417 express from my inner world, which makes me feel perfect.” Similarly, Respondent 5 (R05) felt that
418 meta-talk could enhance the sense of realism in a conversation: “It feels a bit like a person, able to
419 self-reflect.” Similarly, Respondent 12 (R12) also believed that continuous conversation can
420 improve the sense of realism: “Maintaining continuity feels like consulting a long-term, permanent
421 psychologist or companion.” However, some respondents pointed out that many types of relational
422 cues had no effect or even had a negative impact. For instance, R12 noted, “Its self-disclosure is
423 still just a program.” R14 felt that the humor cue was limited: “It tells some dry jokes, the kind that
424 are standard.”
425 A one-way repeated measures ANOVA was conducted to investigate differences in the
426 experience of specific types of relational cues, revealing a significant main effect of cue type (F (4,
427 96) = 3.69, p = 0.003, η ² = 0.16). Specifically, empathy (M = 8.76, SD = 1.01) was rated

429 continuity (M = 7.40, SD = 1.58) (ps < 0.012), while no other pairwise differences were statistically

431 impact on emotional interaction to some extent, empathy is particularly effective.
432 3.3. Discussion
433 Study 2 further explored the effects of self-disclosure, empathy, humor, continuity, and meta-talk
434 on emotional interaction experiences through qualitative online survey methods. Empathy,
435 consistent with Study 1, plays a relatively positive role in emotional interaction experiences, while
436 other types of relational cues exhibit more complex effects.
437 Based on the findings we propose a model for the user’s emotional experience process in
438 human-AI emotional interaction, showcasing the role of various relational cue types (see Figure 6).
439 Specifically, users first express their emotional feelings, then receive understanding from the AI
440 (relying on the AI’s cognitive empathy ability), perceive the relational cues presented by the AI,
441 and ultimately experience a better emotional outcome. In this process, relational cues can be
442 categorized into two types, each with different pathways of influence. First, emotional interaction
443 cues, including self-disclosure, empathy, and humor, primarily enhance the depth of emotional
444 interaction. Second, dialogue logic cues, including meta-talk and continuity, primarily improve the
445 coherence and fluency of the conversation.

449 4. General Discussion
450 This research revealed that chatbot relational cues significantly impact human-AI emotional
451 interaction experiences, with this effect moderated by individual mind perception and influenced by
452 the intensity of negative emotional events. Specifically, Study 1, employing experimental methods,
453 showed that relational cues can enhance emotional interaction experiences. In particular, empathy
454 and continuity play key positive roles. For individuals with low mind perception, the impact of
455 relational cues was more pronounced, while for individuals with high mind perception, the impact
456 was less pronounced. Furthermore, the effects of relational cue’s specific types were greater in
457 extreme negative emotional events than in moderate negative emotional events. Study 2, using
458 online survey methods, further explored the multidimensional effects of relational cue and its
459 specific types on emotional interaction experiences, such as enhancing the authenticity of the
460 conversation, increasing intimacy, and improving conversation quality. Empathy still demonstrated
25
--- Page 29 ---
461 relatively the most positive effect. Overall, based on the multi-dimensional perspective, this research
462 offers a reference for optimizing the emotional support model for chatbots.
463 In general, the relational cues exhibited by chatbots improved human-AI emotional interaction
464 experiences to a certain extent (significantly improving arousal, usability, emotional attachment,
465 and human-AI trust, and marginally significant improving pleasure) , thus answering Q1. This
466 finding is consistent with prior research showing that relational agents can effectively improve
467 individuals’ emotional states (Chiauzzi et al., 2024; Sha et al., 2024) and are often more preferred
468 by users compared to task-oriented agents (Bickmore & Picard, 2005). Moreover, this result
469 supports the CASA paradigm and social presence theory, which suggest that people treat computers
470 as social actors when they display relational cues, thereby increasing the sense of connection and
471 engagement (Nass et al., 1994; Weng et al., 2025). According to the qualitative findings in Study 2,
472 respondents repeatedly noted that relational cues made chatbots appear seem more humanlike,
473 leading them to perceive the interaction as an emotional engagement with a human (R07: “I feel
474 that, at this moment, it’s not just a robot, but more like a companion by my side”). This may also be
475 one of the key reasons why relational cues enhance individuals’ emotional interaction experiences
476 (Weng et al., 2025; Zhang & Rau, 2023). These findings further contribute to the application of the
477 CASA paradigm and social presence theory in the human-AI relationship field.
478 It is worth noting that study 1 found that chatbot relational cues did not significantly affect
479 dominance. This finding may be explained by the nature of dominance itself. Dominance refers to
480 the ability of individuals to actively shift negative emotional states and sustain positive emotional
481 states. It primarily depends on a deep cognitive understanding of one’s own emotional responses
26
--- Page 30 ---
482 (Gratch & Marsella, 2004), which may rely more on individual introspection than on the
483 characteristics of a chatbot. This aspect warrants further exploration.
484 When examining the specific types of relational cues, it is evident that empathy cues exhibited
485 by chatbots had the most significant effects in both studies, influencing all aspects of emotional
486 interaction experience. This result aligns with the majority of previous studies (Inzlicht et al., 2024;
487 Liao & Yan, 2022; Liu & Sundar, 2018; Liu et al., 2024). However, respondents in Study 2
488 consistently reported that empathy enhanced perceived authenticity, which does not support the
489 finding that empathy may reduce authenticity and produce a counterproductive effect (Seitz, 2024).
490 In particular, from the perspective of cognition, empathy plays a key role in understanding users’
491 emotional states, enabling the chatbot to express appropriate relational cues (Schurz et al., 2021).
492 When individuals experience negative emotions, AI can alleviate these feelings by expressing care,
493 understanding, and comfort (Pentina et al., 2023) (R07: “It can understand some of the things I want
494 to express from my inner world, which makes me feel perfect”). From the perspective of emotion,
495 AI’s ability to imitate human emotions is key to fostering emotional resonance, which significantly
496 enhances the emotional interaction experiences, such as pleasure and arousal (Liu et al., 2024). In
497 addition, continuity was found to play an important role in Study 1, significantly improving
498 emotional attachment and usability, which aligns with the findings of Go and Sundar (2019). By
499 acknowledging prior disclosures, continuity enhances user engagement (Rheu et al., 2024; Sundar
500 et al., 2016), fostering deeper emotional discussions and helping users find emotional support across
501 multiple interactions, which in turn improves usability. Additionally, continuity makes users feel
502 valued (R10: “If the conversation remains continuous, I feel like it is really listening to me”), which
27
--- Page 31 ---
503 further fosters emotional attachment. However, in Study 2, participants highlighted the complex
504 effect of continuity on emotional interaction experiences, noting that its positive impact was less
505 stable than that of empathy.
506 As for the effects of other types of relational cues, the findings suggest that self-disclosure
507 displayed by chatbot significantly decreased perceived dominance. This may be due to the relatively
508 superficial nature of the information disclosed, which led users to question the authenticity of the
509 chatbot (R12: “Its self-disclosure is still just a program”), thus disrupting their emotional processing
510 (Chung & Kang, 2023). However, this does not necessarily mean that AI chatbot self-disclosure is
511 inherently harmful. When specific factors in the interaction—such as conversational dynamics, user
512 characteristics, and contextual conditions—are taken into account (Papneja & Yadav, 2025), self-
513 disclosure may also significantly enhance the interaction experiences (see Table S13). Humor had
514 a positive impact on arousal, consistent with previous research (Dybala et al., 2009), which could
515 be attributed to stress reduction. However, some respondents reported that the chatbot’s humor was
516 perceived as limited (R10: “Its jokes don’t make me laugh”), which may diminish its effectiveness
517 in enhancing emotional interaction experiences. Finally, meta-talk significantly increased human–
518 AI trust, in line with prior studies (Schuetzler et al., 2020). This could be because meta-talk promotes
519 trust by increasing interaction depth (R22: “This reflective dialogue makes the conversation more
520 profound”) and enhancing social presence(R20: “The dialogue felt less stiff and more natural”).
521 Study 1 found that individuals’ mind perception positively predicted emotional interaction
522 experiences and moderated the impact of relational cues on pleasure, arousal, dominance, emotional
523 attachment, and usability, responding to Q2. The positive effects of mind perception can be
28
--- Page 32 ---
524 explained by the human-AI interaction model outlined by Pataranutaporn et al. (2023), which
525 suggests that individuals’ initial positive beliefs about AI can improve emotional experiences.
526 Notably, the study found that lower mind perception levels were associated with bigger effects of
527 relational cues on emotional interaction experiences, which aligns with the findings of Liu and
528 Sundar (2018). According to Expectancy Violation Theory (EVT, Rheu et al., 2024), positive
529 expectancy violations can lead to more favorable communication outcomes. Compared to
530 individuals with high mind perception, those with low mind perception tend to have lower
531 expectations regarding the chatbot’s psychological abilities. When they encounter a chatbot
532 exhibiting high relational cues, they are more likely to experience surprise due to the chatbot’s
533 human-like characteristics (i.e., positive expectancy violation), which increases their emotional
534 interaction experience.
535 Study 1 classified emotional events based on the intensity of negative emotion and answered
536 Q3: specific types of relational cues have a stronger effect on emotional interaction experiences in
537 extreme negative emotional events compared to moderate negative emotional events. For instance,
538 in extreme negative emotional events, empathy significantly enhanced emotional interaction
539 experiences, while in moderate negative emotional events, these effects weakened or even became
540 nonsignificant. This result may arise from differences in emotional needs and behaviors in various
541 emotional events (Cyders & Smith, 2008): in extreme negative emotion events, individuals often
542 have a stronger desire for emotional understanding and resonance (Pauw et al., 2024), placing
543 greater emphasis on the relational aspects of interaction, which makes relational cues especially
544 self-disclosure and empathy more effective. In contrast, in moderate negative emotion events, users
29
--- Page 33 ---
545 tend to use cognitive reappraisal strategies to regulate their emotions (Kozubal et al., 2023) and may
546 prioritize the instrumental aspects of interaction, such as task information quality, rather than
547 relational cues.
548 4.1 Theoretical contribution
549 This research makes several important contributions to the field of AI psychology. First, it focuses
550 on the increasingly important variable of relational cues and systematically analyzes both the
551 quantitative and qualitative effect of relational cues on emotional interaction experiences. This
552 perspective enriches the human-AI relationship-based theory (Weng et al., 2025), highlighting the
553 crucial role of human cues at the emotional and social level in shaping both human-AI emotional
554 interactions and relationship. Second, this research deepens our understanding of mind perception
555 in human-AI emotional interactions. Beyond confirming that people’s beliefs about AI’s
556 psychological abilities can enhance emotional experience (Pataranutaporn et al., 2023), we found
557 an interesting twist: these beliefs may also reduce the positive effects of relational cues. This double-
558 edged sword effect shows the complex role of mind perception and broadens the application of the
559 three-factor theory of anthropomorphism and mind perception theory in human-AI interaction
560 (Epley et al., 2007; Gray et al., 2007). Third, this study is the first to examine the role of emotional
561 event intensity in human–AI interaction, indicating that interaction experiences are shaped not only
562 by the characteristics of AI chatbots and user traits, but also by contextual factors (i.e., emotional
563 intensity of interactive content). Finally, this research is the first to approach the topic from the
564 multi-dimensional perspective, using a mixed-method approach to systematically explore the effects
565 of relational cue and its specific types on emotional interaction experiences, overcoming the
30
--- Page 34 ---
566 limitations of previous research that relied on a singular perspective. This integrated approach offers
567 a more comprehensive understanding of the mechanisms behind relational cues across different
568 users and events.
569 4.2 Practical contribution
570 Based on these findings, future developers may flexibly design and optimize chatbots’ relational
571 cues and its specific types to enhance the quality of emotional interactions. More specifically, the
572 system can incorporate automated text analysis techniques — such as emotion recognition, emotion
573 classification (Bilquise et al., 2022), semantic context recognition (Pooja et al., 2025) — to enable
574 more adaptive and context-sensitive responses. When emotion recognition detects that users are
575 expressing negative emotional experiences, the chatbot should dynamically display an appropriate
576 level of relational cues, particularly empathy and continuity. In addition, when the analysis indicates
577 that users demonstrate low levels of mind perception or express extremely negative emotional states,
578 the system should further increase the presence and intensity of relational cues to provide stronger
579 emotional support and foster greater emotional resonance.
580 4.3 Limitations and future directions
581 This research has several limitations. First, in Study 1, participants engaged in short-term emotional
582 interactions, which means the results only capture the immediate effects of relational cue and its
583 specific types. This makes it challenging to evaluate their long-term influence or the development
584 of sustained emotional attachment and human–AI trust. Future research should explore the role of
585 chatbot relational cues over extended periods. For example, it is important to explore whether
586 these cues might lead to addiction, have adverse effects on real-life intimate relationships, or trigger
31
--- Page 35 ---
587 intense emotional attachment to the chatbot (Shank et al., 2025; Xie & Pentina, 2022), Such research
588 could inform the development of chatbots with different roles that incorporate appropriate relational
589 cues, such as ad-hoc supporters, temporary assistants, and persistent companions (Nißen et al.,
590 2022).
591 Second, Study 1 employed a post hoc classification method to manipulate emotional event
592 intensity, which suffers from low replication and insufficient continuity of variables. Future research
593 should consider pre-manipulation to improve the precision of the study design and the
594 interpretability of the results.
595 Third, the participants recruited through social media in both studies were relatively young
596 adults. Prior research has shown that different age groups may have distinct expectations regarding
597 the usability of AI chatbots (Manigandan & Alur, 2024; Wang & Lo, 2025). These preferences
598 highlight the importance of considering age-related differences in the design and evaluation of AI
599 chatbots. To address this limitation, future research should explore the effects of relational cues
600 across different age groups and verify the cross-population validity of the conclusions.
601 Finally, at the data collection level, Study 1 relied on self-report measures, which might have
602 introduced self-report bias. Study 2 required respondents to recall the role of relational cues during
603 emotional interactions with AI, which might have introduced recall bias. Respondents’ descriptions
604 of the effects of relational cues might also have varied depending on the specific contexts of their
605 recalled experiences (e.g., the type of negative emotion or the content of their disclosure). Future
606 research could incorporate more objective behavioral indicators to complement self-report measures
607 and more accurately assess emotional interaction experiences. Moreover, adopting Ecological
32
--- Page 36 ---
608 Momentary Assessment (EMA) could capture users’ interactions with AI in near real time and
609 natural settings, thereby reducing recall bias and clarifying how variations in negative emotional
610 experiences shape their responsiveness to relational cues.
611 5. Conclusion
612 The key conclusions of this research, which was conducted among young adults, are as follows:
613 First, relational cue and its specific types can enhance emotional interaction experiences to a certain
614 degree. Second, mind perception moderates the influence of relational cues on pleasure, arousal,
615 dominance, emotional attachment and usability; higher levels of mind perception are associated
616 with weaker effects of relational cues on pleasure, arousal, dominance, emotional attachment and
617 usability. Third, in extreme negative emotional events, the impact of relational cue’s specific types
618 (particularly empathy) on emotional interaction experiences is greater compared to that in moderate
619 negative emotional events.

2.Study 1: Pre-experiment on the Effectiveness of Manipulating Relational
Cues in Chatbots
2.1. Participants
To select participants who could accurately perceive different levels of relational cues, 23
psychology/applied psychology students were recruited for this study. After excluding 5 participants
who completed the tasks in less than 2 minutes or answered in a regular pattern, 18 valid participants
remained, with an average age of 20.78 years (SD = 1.73), including 11 males and 7 females.
2.2. Method
The experiment used a within-subjects design with a single factor (high relational cues vs. low
relational cues). Participants first read brief text materials to understand the basic concept of
relational cues. They were then asked to rate the extent to which AI showed relational cues in 12
2
--- Page 47 ---
randomly presented human-AI dialogues. The rating scale ranged from 1 (none at all) to 5 (a lot).
The generation method of human-AI dialogues was as follows:
In the “Doubao” app, under the “Create AI Agent” feature, the following text prompts were used:
• For the high relational cues group, the prompt was: You need to help users alleviate
emotional distress. The user will confide in you emotionally, and you should show
human-like emotions and social behaviors in the conversation, focusing on five aspects:
empathy, self-disclosure, humor, meta-talk, and continuity. The specific definition are as
follows...
• For the low relational cues group, the prompt was: You need to help users alleviate
emotional distress. The user will confide in you emotionally, and you do not need to show
any human-like emotions or social behaviors (such as self-disclosure, empathy, humor,
meta-talk, and continuity) in the conversation. The specific definition are as follows...
Subsequently, the experimenter simulated scenarios like being scolded by a leader, having a
dispute with a friend, or being ignored by parents, and participants had conversations with both
types of chatbots, generating 12 dialogues in total, with 6 dialogues for each type (see Figure 3).
2.3. Results
The results of the Wilcoxon signed-rank test showed that participants rated the perception of high
relational cue dialogues (M = 4.00, SD = 0.61) significantly higher than that of low relational cue
dialogues (M = 1.57, SD = 1.12), Z = -3.64, p < 0.001. This indicates that text prompts can effectively
manipulate the level of relational cues displayed by the chatbot.
