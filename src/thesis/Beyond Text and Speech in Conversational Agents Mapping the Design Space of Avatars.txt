Beyond Text and Speech in Conversational Agents: Mapping the Design Space of Avatars

DIS '24: Designing Interactive Systems Conference, IT University of Copenhagen, Denmark, July 2024

Conversational agents have gained widespread popularity due to their ability to simulate and sustain contextual conversations. Prior works predominantly focused on computational challenges. However, avatars — the representation of the agent — impact user interactions and perception of conversational agents’ trustworthiness and usefulness. Despite their importance, we lack a holistic understanding of conversational agent avatar design space. In this work, we address this gap by defining a categorization of 10 dimensions that is based on the analysis and iterative coding of 266 conversational agent papers from 160 venues spanning 2003 to the present. In addition, we built an interactive browser to facilitate exploration and interaction with these dimensions and their interrelationships. Our categorization lays the groundwork for researchers, designers, and practitioners to discern task-specific and contextual aspects of conversational agent avatar design. Our work fosters innovative ideas to facilitate new interactions with avatars by surfacing current patterns and highlighting open challenges.

1 INTRODUCTION
Conversational agents are becoming commonplace in many domains including banking [156], e-commerce [59], healthcare [30] as virtual assistants [68], digital coaches [197] and mental health support agents [72] due to their ability to simulate and sustain contextual human-like conversations [135]. In the last decade, conversational agents have seen significant advancements, particularly in overcoming computational challenges related to Natural Language Understanding (NLU) and Processing (NLP) [43] including, contextual understanding [172], intent identification [154], ambiguity resolution [41], and multimodal interactions [111].

To facilitate engaging, transparent, and contextual conversations between a user and an agent and to create a more engaging dialogue between these two parties, AI-enabled conversational agents often accompany an avatar. In the context of conversational agents, an avatar is a representation of the agent in a digital environment [109, 213]. The design of avatars varies from simple objects [129] to robot-like [104] to animal-like [121] to human-like [54] that can be static or interactive. Some of them can even simulate expressions [69], provide dialogue cues [207], and convey the information needed to sustain a conversation [16]. Avatars and their different characteristics, such as human likeness and animation, can make the agent appear more natural and authentic, resulting in a more personal relationship with the users that can lead to increased user satisfaction and trust in the conversational agents [82, 85, 108].

Prior works suggest that the design of conversational agents’ avatars, significantly impacts users perceived usefulness, trustworthiness, and expertise of the agent [69, 82, 137]. For example, in healthcare [62] and virtual service [45], avatars that present animated visual cues [62] and facial expressions [45] can help users to reflect their emotions in conversations and enable agents to offer emotional support. An avatar's facial expressions and posture may also influence how a user would be comfortable assigning significant social and task attraction to the agent, reinforcing the level of trust the user places in the agent's competence [95].

Despite the importance of avatar design and the role it plays in facilitating human-AI conversation [34, 60, 178], less attention has been paid to synthesizing and characterizing the avatar design space. Prior works explored some specific aspects such as the design differences between general or domain-specific conversational agents [89], whether the agent can engage users in a single one-to-one or group conversation [185], and the length of user interaction with the agent [162]. However, we still lack a comprehensive categorization that systematically organizes the current knowledge of critical avatar design dimensions, their interrelationships, and future implications.

In this work, we address this gap by mapping the current conversational agent avatar design space, cataloging existing conversational agents, and categorizing avatar design across various design dimensions (see Figure 1). Previous research defines a design space as a conceptual construct comprising distinct independent parts, each representing a specific design decision and their interplay  [184]. The combination of design choices across these independent parts forms the design space, allowing the representation of existing solutions and the exploration of novel combinations yet to be discovered. We began by compiling a database of conversational agent research papers across 160 venues from the past 20 years, published in ACM Digital Library (ACM DL) [1], ScienceDirect [7], and IEEE Xplore [4]. To compile our database, we performed keyword searches using “chatbot” and “conversational agent” to identify relevant papers, which resulted in 1272 papers for “chatbot” and 8349 for “conversational agent”. After removing papers that did not mention a chatbot with an avatar, our final database consists of a total of 266 papers. Informed by prior work, we started deductively with a set of six guiding questions (GQs) to guide our attention towards important avatar design considerations. We then inductively developed new codes and iterated on the codes as we sifted through our data. Six members of our research group perused each paper, coded, and iteratively refined the categories that led to the final set of 10 design dimensions including, Appearance, Gender, Motion, Interaction, Dimensionality, Input Modality, Conversation Type, Type of Device, Number of Agents, and Application. In addition, we built an interactive browser to facilitate exploration and interaction with these dimensions and their interrelationships1. The browser assists searching and filtering relevant papers by publication year, keyword search, and design dimensions. Our interactive browser also provides a summary of the design dimensions, a set of visualizations to explore current trends and identify design gaps. Furthermore, the interactive browser allows researchers and practitioners to add their research papers on avatars that will dynamically update our categorization and visualizations.

To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to develop a categorization for the conversational agent avatar design space. Our work contributes a foundation for researchers, designers, and practitioners to identify critical design aspects, discern relationships between them, and experiment with novel combinations of the various aspects that can support avatar design based on different contexts. This is specifically pertinent for the DIS community, as it provides a categorization to design more nuanced and contextually appropriate conversational agents. Our work fosters innovative ideas for understanding and improving novel interactions with avatars pushing the boundaries of avatar interaction design beyond the confines of text and speech. By revealing patterns and identifying potential research gaps, we motivate fresh design concepts aimed at enhancing user experience, trust, and the perceived usefulness of agents. We also discuss open challenges, including ethical considerations for conversational avatar design, which provide a pathway for future work on this important topic. Ultimately, our efforts aim to cultivate more accessible and inclusive conversational agent design practices.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In the next section, we describe the background of conversational agents, avatar design, and existing challenges of categorizing avatar design. Section 3 describes our paper collection, iterative coding, and categorization of design space. Afterwards, we discuss our design dimensions and their subcategories in Section 4. We then discuss the results of our analysis to identify patterns and relationships within our categorization in Section 5. Section 6 discusses the implications of our categorization, insights extracted from our categorization, suggestions for future directions for conversational agent avatar design, and limitations of our categorization. Finally, we conclude this paper in Section 7.

2 RELATED WORK
Conversational agents have gained popularity in academic and research fields as virtual assistants [11], customer support [163], digital coaches [197] and healthcare agents [72] due to their ability to hold contextual human-like conversations with users [10, 70, 115]. Prior work has shown how avatar design considerations impact user experiences while conversing with agents [82]. In this section, we discuss prior works and advancements made in conversational agent development during the last decades, the design of conversational agent avatars, and existing methods and challenges of categorizing avatar design.

2.1 Advances in Conversational Agent Development
Early conversational agents, such as ELIZA [216] and Parry [64] used pattern-matching algorithms to match the user queries with a predefined user intent to provide an appropriate response. Such algorithms relied on rule-based user intent identification and did not account for the conversation context, resulting in inaccuracies in identifying user intentions [9]. Modern conversational systems have improved as Machine Learning (ML) algorithms have advanced [38]. Conversational agents can now predict a user's intentions using the entire conversation context, resulting in a more natural conversation [176, 187]. This allowed the use of conversational agents in various applications like customer service [83], restaurant ordering systems [131], personal assistants (e.g., Amazon Alexa and Google Home) [171], managing social media marketing [114], learning assistants in the education sector [192], etc. Furthermore, the rise of conversational agent development platforms such as Facebook Messenger [3], Dialogflow [2], and Juji [5] enabled designers and practitioners to create conversational agents faster than traditional methods that include coding an agent from scratch. These platforms contributed to the massive expansion in the number of available conversational systems capable of high-level computation [206], user intent identification [147], and large-scale data collection [140].

2.2 Conversational Agent Avatar Design and its Impact on User Experience
Recent research brought attention to conversational agent avatar design by highlighting the avatars’ impact on how users perceive a conversational agent [82]. A user's perception of the agent's abilities significantly impacts user experience [60, 228] and trust [60, 204], making avatar design considerations critical. Avatars often provide visual cues through facial expression and body language, which have been shown to be effective in communicating emotion and empathy during a conversation [67]. For example, avatars in personal assistants such as Kuki (formerly known as Mitsuku) [6], Xiaoice [236], and Ellie [74] can change their expressions based on the user's response and the conversation context. These facial expressions, when combined with the conversational agent's stories, jokes, and songs, effectively entertained users while also communicating emotions such as happiness, sadness, and kindness [117].

Furthermore, surveys on social conversational agents have shown that the lack of social characteristics and personalization in a conversational agent often leads to user frustration and dissatisfaction [53, 190]. Social characteristics can be integrated into a conversational agent by designing avatars and visual cues that are appropriate to the conversation. For example, a healthcare agent's social cues may help the agent develop interpersonal connections with the user, resulting in greater user satisfaction [161]. Moreover, users are often less forgiving of conversational AI when compared to humans [18]. Research showed that the presence of visual cues in a conversational agent avatar helps users be more lenient and willing to forgive an agent's natural language processing mistakes [90]. However, designing a conversational agent avatar is not trivial as different combinations of visual cues may have varying effects on user satisfaction, and too many visual cues can lead to user dissatisfaction [19, 210].

Similar to aforementioned domains, in areas where conversational agents are designed and deployed to help users make decisions, such as e-commerce and customer service, avatars and their visual cues have been shown to influence users’ purchase intentions [65] and how long they interact with the conversational agent [78]. However, contrary to the aforementioned domains, in these domains, excessive use of human-like features in conversational agent avatars can result in negative user satisfaction [65]. These studies highlight the importance of further exploration of the conversational agent avatar design space to categorize them into groups that can help researchers and practitioners identify appropriate avatar designs for their tasks, domains, and applications.

2.3 Existing Categorization of Conversational Agents
Researchers in the conversational agent domain have developed various categorizations for different aspects of the conversations between users and agents. For example, Nißen et al. proposed a categorization based on the amount of time users spend during social interactions with conversational agents [162]. Based on conversational agents’ temporal profiles, they abstracted three time-dependent design archetypes including ad-hoc supporters, temporary assistants, and persistent companions across 22 dimensions. Seering et al. explored conversational agents’ social roles and conversational abilities beyond dyadic one-on-one communication by reviewing both commercial and independent conversational agents [185]. Following a research-through-design approach, they proposed seven new conceptual categories of conversational agents: Antagonist, Archivist, Authority figure, Dependent, Clown, Social Organizer, and Storyteller. Feine et al. proposed a taxonomy that identified social cues into four major categories, including verbal, visual, auditory, and invisible [79]. Diederich et al. analyzed 51 conversational agent platforms and categorized conversational agents into three archetypes, including multi-language, general-purpose, and text-based [75] across 11 dimensions. However, the categorization of conversational avatar design across design dimensions remains underexplored. In this paper, we propose a systematic categorization for the classification of conversation agent avatar design across various design dimensions to help researchers and practitioners who work in the domain of conversation agent design and development to identify context- and task-appropriate avatar design.

3 METHODOLOGY
In this section, we describe our methodology, including the details of our paper selection process, iterative coding, and categorization of design space, resulting in 10 design dimensions (see Table 1).

3.1 Paper Selection
We created a database of conversational agent papers from January 2003 to June 2023 across 160 venues from ACM Digital Library (ACM DL), ScienceDirect, and IEEE Xplore, including papers published in premier venues on conversational agents design and research such as CHI, IUI, CSCW, CUI, and IVA. We began searching for papers by performing partial keyword searches [28], which also checked for keyword fragments, using four keywords — “chatbot”, “conversational agent”, “assistant”, and “dialogue” — in paper titles, where the results returned papers where the title had words that partially matched the search keywords. To perform these searches, we used the respective search engines embedded with the publication venues. The “chatbot” keyword resulted in 1272 papers, the “conversational agent” keyword resulted in 8349 papers, the keyword “assistant” resulted in 7541 papers, and the dialogue” resulted in 4945 papers. In ACM DL, we discovered 305, 8063, 3855, and 1276 papers corresponding to “chatbot”,“conversational agent”, “assistant”, and “dialogue” respectively. In contrast, ScienceDirect contained relatively fewer papers, with 266 papers for “chatbot”, 93 papers for “conversational agent”, 2057 papers for “assistant”, and 1764 papers for “dialogue”. Lastly, our keyword search in IEEE Xplore revealed 707 papers under the “chatbot” keyword and 93 papers for “conversational agent”, 1629 papers for “assistant”, and 1905 papers for “dialogue”.

Two members of our research team independently skimmed each paper to check whether the conversational agent had a visual interface and filtered the ones that did not. During the filtering, we found that the papers that we collected using the “assistant” and “dialogue” keywords either lacked a visual interface or were covered by the other two keywords. Subsequently, we discarded these redundant papers from our categorization. After the initial filtering, we had 364 papers in total, 151 papers from the “chatbot” search and 213 papers from the “conversational agent” search. Next, we distributed these 364 papers among six members of our research team, who read through the papers and identified whether these papers contained an avatar and filtered papers that did not have an avatar. At the end of this filtering process, our database consisted of a total of 266 papers (see Figure 2), 144 papers for “chatbot” and 122 papers for “conversational agent”.

3.2 Coding
We started our data coding process by deriving an initial codebook based on prior works that explored how the design of conversational agents, especially avatars, can impact user satisfaction, trust, and transparency [45, 62, 82, 85, 93, 95, 108, 188]. In addition, we considered potential design dimensions in conversational agents that impacts avatar design.

We combined both deductive and inductive approaches to coding. We started deductively with a set of guiding questions (GQs), but then inductively [152, 191] developed new codes and iterated on the codes as we sifted through our data. The lead author began by reviewing the titles, abstracts, and visual interface figures of the 266 papers in the database to identify an initial set of three GQs regarding the avatar appearance, interactivity, and application. The research team then met to discuss these potential dimensions and the existing literature on avatar categorizations [75, 79, 162, 185]. After discussions and deliberations, the research team created six GQs to guide our attention toward important avatar design dimensions. The GQs are as follows:

GQ1: What are the different avatar features that have been used in current conversational agent literature? The characteristics or attributes of an avatar that contribute to its design based on its visual features, manifestation, and implementation. For instance, an avatar feature defines how it looks visually or how its appearance transforms in response to user interactions [121, 236].

GQ2: What input modalities are commonly used to communicate with conversational agents? Input modalities are the different methods or modes through which users interact or input information to the conversational agent. The differences in input modality could influence the type of avatar features. For example, in a voice-based conversational agent, visual cues may also be incorporated to enhance the user experience and provide additional cues for comprehension [177, 232].

GQ3: What is the conversation structure between the user and the conversational agent? Different types of conversation can affect the avatar's features. For instance, conversations can be through a pre-defined set of options to communicate with the agent or have a free-form conversation. Prior works show that animated avatars are more likely to facilitate such natural conversation types [143, 215].

GQ4: What are the different devices for deploying conversational systems? The appearance of the avatar might be influenced by the platform or technology used to deliver the conversational agent's experience. For example, avatars for virtual assistants on smartphones might be optimized for smaller screens, while those in virtual reality environments might be more elaborate [151, 231].

GQ5: What is the number of agents used in conversational systems? If the system has multiple agents, the avatars may need distinct appearances to differentiate them, and their visual characteristics may need alternative designs to create the impression of group interaction [109, 237].

GQ6: What are the most prominent applications of conversational systems? Application refers to the context or specific domain in which the avatar is being used, and this could range from commercial and non-commercial domains such as education, medicine, and e-commerce. Different applications necessitate distinct avatar designs; for instance, in customer service applications, the avatar's appearance and behavior may emphasize straightforward communication and problem-solving skills [16, 144].

We distributed the collection of 266 papers among six members of our research team. The first author reviewed and coded 141 papers and randomly distributed the remaining 125 papers among the other five members. The first four members coded 20 papers each based on their time and availability, and the fifth member coded 45 papers. We used a spreadsheet application for coding with a separate sheet for each member of the coding team. All members carefully perused their assigned papers and coded them based on the guiding questions. During the coding process, the team members were diligent about identifying new codes that emerged during their coding process. They entered and maintained their codes on their assigned spreadsheet. All members of the research team including the coders met once a week for a total of 16 weeks to discuss the papers they had read and discuss and consolidate new codes. This process resulted in new codes and potential design dimensions for our proposed categorization. During the weekly sessions, we resolved coding conflicts through iterative discussions until we reached a consensus. We also reviewed, discussed, and consolidated the codes to preserve their consistency throughout this 16-week coding process. Throughout this coding process and iterative discussion with the entire team, we finalized 10 design dimensions and their aspects to categorize 266 conversational agent papers in our categorization.

4 CATEGORIZATION OF AVATAR DESIGN
The ten final design dimensions for our categorization of avatar design are: Appearance, Gender, Motion, Interaction, Dimensionality, Input Modality, Conversation Type, Type of Device, Number of Agents, and Application. In this section, we describe these design dimensions and their subcategories.

4.1 Appearance
We refer to Appearance as the external look that constitutes the manifestation of a conversational agent's visual features. We came up with four subcategories in this design dimension — Human-like, Robotic, Object, and Animal-like. Human-like avatars are the most common avatars found in our paper database. They resemble the characteristics of a human being [56, 181]. For example, Mei represents a young Asian female agent with eyes, nose, lips, and semi-casual clothing. She also sports a bow tie and has a short hairstyle [56]. In contrast, robotic avatars resemble a machine without expressions. However, they are sometimes associated with some human features [203, 225]. For example, StudyBuddy is an agent with a mechanical rectangular face with two eyes without irises, an open, smiling mouth, and a pair of antennas on top of its mechanical ears [203]. The object avatars resemble stationary inanimate items, such as fruits, company logos, or a globe [139, 159]. For example, MSABot is an agent with a visual appearance of the top half of a pineapple with an orange body and green leaves [139]. Finally, animal-like avatars resemble the appearance of an animal, such as a bird, fish, or a cat [23, 57]. For instance, Max is a conversational agent that is represented by a blue cartoon-like whale with a geyser of water being spouted out of its blowhole [23]. Max exemplifies an avatar represented by an aquatic animal.

4.2 Gender
Many chatbot avatars represent a variety of genders through their physical manifestation. Due to the complexities that gender categorization might introduce, we followed the gender that has been reported specifically by the respective authors in their published papers. Based on that, we have four subcategories for gender — Female, Male, Gender Neutral, and NA. An example of a female avatar is Yeonhee, who is represented as a woman wearing a long-sleeve blue rib-knit top and a dark blue skirt [180]. Brad is a male avatar, represented as a middle-aged man with short brown hair, well-defined cheekbones, and a neutral facial expression [167]. An example of a gender-neutral avatar is PocketBot which is a speech bubble with two eyes without irises looking through a visor and a beeping antenna [234]. In instances where the gender of the avatar was not explicitly mentioned, we refrained from making assumptions and instead used the designation "non-applicable" (NA) [118, 217].

4.3 Motion
We refer to Motion as a visual representation of movements in avatars. Based on the presence or absence of motion, we categorized papers into two subcategories — Static and Animated. A static avatar does not have motions and is visually represented as a still image [116, 123]. For example, Andy uses a static human-like female avatar that shows no visual movement throughout the conversation [116]. In contrast, an animated avatar represents motion through visual movement during a conversation [12, 100]. For example, Hernández-Trapote et al. demonstrated a human-like male conversational agent who performs hand gestures and physical movements to express itself [100].

4.4 Interaction
We consider to Interaction as the manipulation of avatars via user inputs such as mouse hover, click, and keyboard entry. We have two subcategories for interaction — non-interactive and interactive. A non-interactive avatar lacks user interaction capabilities [113, 128]. When a user tries to interact with a non-interactive avatar, it will not provide any response or feedback to such interactions. For example, the avatar in Law et al.’s work presents a static non-interactive representation of the conversational agent, which does not reciprocate a user's attempts to interact with it [128]. In contrast, Moli can be interacted with via hovering which results in Moli jumping up and down as a response [141]. Among the 40 interactive avatars in our database, we found that 26 avatars were equipped with interaction to elicit longer user engagement [141] and provide an immersive human-like experience [73]. We also identified 14 avatars that use interaction to deliver empathy [236], emotion [102], and culturally appropriate hand gestures [49].

4.5 Dimensionality
We attribute Dimensionality as the number of planar dimensions across avatars’ height, width, or depth. We have two subcategories for dimensionality — 2D and 3D. 2D avatars have a length and width in the rendered avatar representation [61, 97]. For example, ProtoChat is represented using a flat image with only horizontal and vertical dimensions (X and Y) in the two-dimensional axis plane [61]. In contrast, 3D avatars have an additional depth dimension (Z-axis) along with length and width [29, 202]. The avatar proposed by Bernsen et al. used a 3D avatar in a three-dimensional environment to show its movement along the Z-axis [29]. By utilizing the 3D avatar, they were able to showcase the avatar's motion in a way that provides valuable insights into its behavior and spatial dynamics.

4.6 Input Modality
We refer to Input Modality as the mechanism used by the conversational agent system to facilitate communication between the user and the agent. We have three different subcategories for this dimension — Text, Voice, and Multimodal. In the text input modality, users can engage in conversations with the agent exclusively through text-based interactions [113, 174]. For example, Prasetyo et al. present a conversational agent that provides a text-box interface where the users can enter or type in their questions and respond to the agent as free-form text [174]. In voice input modality, users can use spoken language as input to communicate with conversational agents [56, 169]. For example, users can communicate with Xiaowei by speaking through a microphone [169]. Multimodal input modality supports more than one input modality such as text, hand-gesture, voice, etc [92, 106]. For example, users can communicate with Mia by using voice through a microphone and performing hand gestures captured by a kinect [92]. We found a total of 44 avatars that support multimodal input. Among them, 31 avatars use voice with text input to offer accessibility [37, 200]. In addition, we found eight avatars that are equipped with cameras to capture nonverbal body movements and provide an immersive conversation experience [92, 148]. We also found four avatars that support multimodal interactions through text and image [127, 195].

4.7 Conversation Type
We use Conversation Type to refer to the form of the conversation that takes place between a user and an agent. We have two subcategories in this design dimension — Free-form, and Structured. A free-form conversation allows the user to respond in an open-ended manner [13, 199]. In free-form conversations, the conversational agent employs NLU to identify the user's intent to respond appropriately. Intent identification NLU is frequently implemented using well-known platforms such as DialogFlow [2] and Rasa [35]. For example, Svikhnushina et al. proposed an agent that can facilitate conversations by allowing users to input free-form text responses using a text box provided in its interface [13]. In contrast, an agent facilitating structured conversations only allows the users to respond with close-ended responses, where the users provide their input by selecting a pre-defined option or with a yes or no. [98, 164]. In structured conversations, users are presented with a set of buttons, each of which indicates a probable user intent. For example, Emily provides the user with a set of possible response options, from which the user can only select one [164].

4.8 Type of Device
We considered Type of Device based on the device(s) supported by the conversational agent. We categorized the types of devices into five subcategories — Smartphone, Desktop Application, Web Application, Cross-Platform, and AR/VR. Smartphone conversational agents are developed for smartphone devices such as Android devices [37, 133]. Desktop application conversational agents are software that runs on personal computers or servers [56, 105]. Web application conversational agents are designed to operate within a web browser, such as Chrome and Firefox [61, 224] Certain web application conversational agents function as browser extensions, such as Juji [224]. Cross-platform conversational agents support more than one device type [52, 234]. For example, PocketBot can be used in smartphones as well as a web application running on a personal computer [234]. Finally, AR/VR conversational agents support Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) environments, where the user can see the conversational agent avatar in a virtual environment [29, 155]. For example, Miyake et al. developed a conversational agent for Q&A, where the avatar can be seen and interacted with in an AR environment [155].

4.9 Number of Agents
We refer to the Number of Agents as the number of distinguishable agents a system provides in a single conversational session. We have two subcategories in this dimension — Single-agent, and Multi-agent. A singular conversational agent participates in the conversation in single-agent conversational systems [26, 173]. For example, in Woebot, users can only communicate with a single agent, “Woebot”, during the conversation [26]. In contrast, Jiang et al. developed a conversational agent system that facilitated conversations with up to three conversational agents regarding the impact of COVID-19 on civic life [109].

4.10 Application
We refer to Application as the context where the conversational agent is expected to run, survive, and serve users. Based on our database, we categorized the papers across seven subcategories — Service, Personal Assistance, Healthcare, Survey, Learning, Moderation, and Journaling. Conversational agents in the service subcategory provide users with task-specific support, such as providing informational assistance [112], order tracking [103], or customer Q&A [125]. For instance, Kuramoto et al. propose a customer support chatbot that engages in friendly conversations to deal with angry customers in customer-support work [125]. Personal assistance conversational agents support users by acting as assistants for their everyday activities [166, 236]. For example, Xiaoice is a conversational agent that provides empathetic and personalized interactions to establish long-term relationships [236]. Healthcare conversational agents are designed to provide health-related information to the user, such as nutrition information and pharmaceutical care [94, 159]. For example, PTSDialogue provides information to people with PTSD on how to regulate their lifestyle [94]. Survey conversational agents, such as data collection agents, are used to gather customer experience in e-commerce or collect personal information from users [181, 224]. For example, AI Minion is an interview chatbot that collects interview applicants’ personal and demographic information [224]. We categorized conversational agents designed to assist students with schoolwork and studies in the learning subcategory [134, 203]. For instance, Edubot helps students learn new languages by assisting them with text-based conversations containing translations from English to other languages [134]. Moderation conversational agents assist the users to facilitate consolidation in group discussions [24, 120]. For example, DebateBot acts as a moderator that collects user inputs from group discussions to help users reach a consensus [120]. Journaling conversational agents are designed to record user information on a daily basis. The user or other individuals can later use the collected information to analyze the changes in habits, emotions, and overall well-being [174, 209]. For example, Foodbot provides users with regular interventions to keep a digital journal of their food intake and provide suggestions to maintain a healthy diet [174].

5 EXPLORING PATTERNS AND RELATIONSHIPS IN OUR CATEGORIZATION
The goal of our analysis process was to identify emergent trends and patterns in avatar design from our dataset. To that end, we first plotted a series of normalized stacked bar charts for each dimension showing the distribution of the number of papers published per year to see the publication trends across our design dimensions, which has been shown to be a successful method of identifying emergent trends in a research field [142]. Second, to investigate the relationships among the design dimensions, we created a confusion matrix [212] of Pearson's correlation [86] as a heatmap with the subcategories of the design dimensions across each axis.

5.1 Investigating Temporal Trends Across Design Dimensions
To analyze the temporal trends across the design dimensions, we plotted a series of normalized stacked bar charts based on the percentage constitution of the subcategories over time (Fig. 3). Each stacked bar represents a year for a design dimension, and the segments in the bar show the normalized proportion of the respective subcategory for that year. With regard to Appearance, we observed an incremental usage of “robotic” avatar appearance with a peak of 57.6% in 2021 and “neutral” Gender with a peak of 51.1% in 2020. This recent peak in 2020 and 2021 could signal a potential trend in using robotic avatars and neutral gender. An example of such an avatar design can be found in Sannon et al.’s work, where they created a conversational system with a robotic appearance and neutral gender to converse with users on issues such as financial stress and household problems [182]. We also found that for “NA” Gender, although their usage is consistent with an average of 28 ± 15% over the past 20 years, there is a significant spike of 83.3% in 2023. The peak in 2023 suggests a substantial shift in designing avatars without mentioning their gender specifics. The use of “NA” has often been justified in research papers that aimed for inclusivity for a diverse user group and high user engagement [73, 84, 109]. For instance, Chris was an NA gender agent built to provide stress management interventions to college students with a diverse demographic that helped avoid gender stereotypes and increased their trust [84]. Furthermore, we observed an upward trend in using chatbots that are static with an average of 30 ± 26% before 2014, which later increased to an average of 70 ± 27% across 2015 to 2023. For instance, before 2015, many research papers from our database used animated avatars to provide visual cues in the avatar to mimic human-like persona and behavior [100, 168]. However, researchers in recent years have opted for static avatars due to their lack of animation, which tends to reduce cognitive load and allows for more focused conversation [183].

Our normalized stacked bar charts also revealed a consistent use of non-interactive Interaction with an average of 67 ± 20% over the past 20 years. This may be due to the positive influence of non-interactive avatars on information credibility and user trust [136, 193]. We also found an increased usage of text Input modality with an average of 63 ± 23% whereas voice and multimodal Input modality were largely underexplored with a peak of 60% in 2013 and 66.7% in 2010 respectively. Prior research suggests that text input modality tends to improve the conversational agent's dialogue processing speed [20, 31], while voice and multimodal input require more computation and often slow down the chatbot conversation delivery [31]. For the Conversation type, we saw an overwhelming usage of free-form Conversation type (85 ± 13% average), which is inclined with prior works that suggest that in most applications, conversational agents are developed for free-flowing natural conversation over being structured (15 ± 12% average) [149]. We also observed that most conversational agents had been largely used as web (31 ± 14% average) and smartphone (38 ± 11% average) applications that could be attributed to their accessibility [80, 227] and economic viability [99]. Despite the availability and accessibility of AR/VR headsets in recent years [177], AR/VR conversational agents are largely underexplored, with a peak of 33.3% back in 2007 and a decline afterward. In terms of the number of agents, we found that an average of 88 ± 17% conversational agents are single agents in our paper database. However, after 2011, several papers have explored multi-agent (21 ± 18%, on average) conversational systems as an alternative to single-agent systems and have seen success in addressing the challenges of user intent identification in a multidomain conversation [109]. Finally, for Applications, we saw a greater number of service (30 ± 16%, on average) conversational agents over other Applications, including learning (20 ± 10%, on average) and personal assistance (30 ± 25%, on average).

5.2 Relationships Between the Design Dimensions
To highlight the correlations among the subcategories of our design dimensions, we calculated Pearson's correlation between them and created a confusion matrix heatmap (Fig. 4). Pearson's correlation is represented by Pearson's correlation coefficient, r (− 1 ≤ r ≤ 1), where − 1 means the weakest correlation and 1 means the strongest correlation. We observed that static avatars with robotic Appearance (r=0.4), noninteractive Interaction (r=0.45), 2D Dimensionality (r=0.84), and text Input modality (r=0.5) are positively correlated. Animated avatars, on the other hand, are positively correlated with human-like Appearance (r=0.52), female Gender (r=0.32), interactive Interaction (r=0.45), 3D Dimensionality (r=0.84), and voice Input modality (r=0.48). For instance, several papers that used human-like avatars with animated Motion and interactive Interaction were found to enable users to express their emotional states more effectively and achieve higher engagement [88, 107, 230]. We also observed that conversational agents with human-like avatars and female Gender effectively provided emotional support and encouraged users to provide more information [116, 226].

In addition, we found that human-like avatars are negatively correlated with neutral Gender (r=-0.51), static Motion (r=-0.52), and 2D Dimensionality (r=-0.51). This suggests that while creating human-like avatars, designers frequently assign them a certain gender, animation, and 3D representation, which has been shown to improve user experience and trust in the conversational agent [173, 180]. For example, Miao et al. demonstrated that in marketing, human-like avatars that are 3D and animated can be more effective in influencing a user's decision in contrast to 2D and static [153]. In contrast, robotic avatars are positively correlated with neutral Gender (r=0.41), static Motion (r=0.4), and 2D Dimensionality (r=0.42). We also observed that robotic avatars are often used for journaling (r=0.14) and survey (r=0.05) Applications that require fast input processing and straightforward communication that can be achieved using 2D Dimensionality and neutral Gender [123, 235, 236]. The avatars designed for desktop applications positively correlated with animated Motion (r=0.36) and 3D Dimensionality (r=0.42). On the other hand, avatars associated with smartphone applications are positively correlated with static Motion (r=0.2) and 2D Dimensionality (r=0.26). Several papers from our database [46, 168, 237] employ animated 3D avatars on desktop applications, noting that the design of such avatars necessitates powerful computational resources to render the avatar, which is infeasible in smartphone applications [214]. Moreover, we observed neither a strong positive nor negative correlation between Application and the other design dimensions, albeit observing a few weak correlations, such as the use of human-like appearance (r=0.14), animation (r=0.22), 3D (r=0.22), and voice input modality (r=0.17) for personal assistants.

Interactive Browser. We built an interactive browser (see Figure 5) that provides an interface to explore our proposed categorization. The interactive browser is implemented as a web application using HTML and JavaScript. We utilized JSON to store the subcategories of the design dimensions and the bibliographical information associated with each paper. The bibliographical information contains the papers’ title, author, publication venue, year, and associated design dimensions.

The interactive browser has three primary sections: the central view (see Figure 5 a), the side panel (see Figure 5 b), and the navigation bar at the top (see Figure 5 c). The central view hosts an array of tiles, represented by thumbnails, each depicting an avatar paper chronologically ordered by publication year. Each thumbnail functions as an interactive button (see Figure 5 d), which reveals details about the corresponding item upon selection and includes a larger picture of the avatar. It also shows a list of subcategory tags, a bibliographical citation, and a BibTeX link, when available. To the left of the central view, a side panel displays the number of papers, followed by a search box. The browser tokenizes any text input into the search box, subsequently executing a keyword search across the bibliographical data and subcategory descriptions linked to each categorization entry. Next to the search field are a series of buttons for filtering papers by time, design dimensions, and their respective subcategories.

At the top, a navigation bar provides access to an array of options, including a “Summary” dialogue box that lists each paper alongside its relevant design dimensions (see Figure 5 e), and a “Heat Map” dialogue box that visualizes Pearson's correlations amongst design dimension subcategories (see Figure 5 f) which allows researchers and practitioners to discern emergent trends within the design space. Furthermore, we have incorporated an “Add Entry” feature to enable users to upload papers that are not present in our browser and assign appropriate subcategories. We plan to moderate new entries for miscategorization and adjust for maintenance for the foreseeable future to keep the browser operational and updated. Finally, a curated list of existing interactive browsers and surveys is accessible for researchers seeking additional exploration in this area of research.

6 DISCUSSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
In this section, we discuss the implications of our proposed categorization of avatar design space and the insights extracted from the categorization and suggest future directions for conversational agent avatar design.

6.1 Implications of our Results
In this paper, we presented a categorization of avatar design, encompassing a wide range of dimensions, from appearance and interactivity to device type and application. The implications of our categorization are multifaceted, including identifying emerging design trends, discerning relationships between the design dimensions, exploring novel combinations of the design dimensions, and informing future avatar design strategies.

6.1.1 Impact of Avatar Appearance, Gender, Motion, and Dimensionality on User Experience and Computational Efficiency. Our Pearson correlation heatmap revealed relationships among avatar appearance, dimensionality, and motion, with a high co-occurrence of robotic avatars with 2D dimensions (r=0.42) and static (r=0.4). We saw that this co-occurrence between 2D dimension and static avatars has prevailed since 1997 till today [25, 40]. What began as an approach to optimize conversational agents’ dialogue processing requirements for low-resource computers [25], has continued to persist due to the focus on optimizing computational performance, such as response time [218], over design elements, such as 3D dimensions (r=-0.42) and animation (r=-0.4). Furthermore, designers may opt for 2D and static design configurations to mitigate the uncanny valley effect [158, 186], where human-like figures that resemble actual humans but are not convincingly realistic are prone to cause discomfort among human observers. This discomfort can be further amplified by cognitive dissonance that occurs when users’ behavioral expectations for an agent are not fulfilled [158, 201]. By opting for human-like designs like 2D and static robotic avatars, designers may negate or avoid such effects [186, 201]. On the other hand, we observed that human-like avatars frequently co-occur with 3D (r=0.51) and animation (r=0.52). In agreement with prior research [110, 189], we argue that employing human-like avatars that are 3D and animated can provide a more immersive experience. In addition to 3D and animation, incorporating identifiable genders, such as female (r=0.51) or male (r=0.38) genders with human-like avatars can promote a personalized user experience by enabling users to relate with the avatar [66] or perceive it as someone they regularly interact with [150, 160]. We argue for careful consideration of the context of use when designing avatars for conversational agents in the future. For instance, while the simplicity afforded by 2D static avatars might mitigate the uncanny valley effect, 3D animated and gender-specific avatars might foster a more immersive and personalized user experience.

6.1.2 Influence of Input Modality on the Design of Animated and 3D Avatars. Our analysis revealed that the input modality, conversation type, and the number of agents impact the chatbot avatar design. For instance, we found that animated and 3D avatars have a positive correlation with voice input modality (r=0.48 and r=0.48) and a negative correlation with text input modality (r=-0.5 and r=-0.48). This observation is corroborated by prior research, which demonstrated that animated and 3D avatars frequently co-occur with voice input modality to provide users with a natural face-to-face human communication interface [145, 198]. Moreover, our analysis showed that animated and 3D avatars are weakly correlated with multimodal input modality (r=0.21 and r=0.18). This is an interesting observation considering that prior works suggest that animated and 3D avatars often support multimodal input to facilitate enhanced functionalities, such as nonverbal communication [194, 219]. For instance, Sonlu et al. showed that nonverbal cues in an animated 3D avatar help to convey the psychological state of the agent, which improved communication with them [194]. However, in several domains, it has been shown that animations can be distracting due to associated motions involved in communicating information [126, 221]. Further research is required to examine how non-verbal multimodal interactions with avatars can increase engagement while balancing distractions.

6.1.3 Weak Correlations Between Avatar Appearance and Conversation Types. We did not find any strong positive or negative correlations between conversation type and the other design dimensions. However, we found some weak correlations between the conversation type and appearance. For instance, our correlation analysis showed that free-form conversations have a weak negative correlation with human-like avatars (r=-0.21) and a weak positive correlation with robotic avatars (r=0.14). Whereas structured conversations have a weak positive correlation with human-like avatars (r=0.21) and a weak negative correlation with robotic avatars (r=-0.14). These weak correlations suggest that designers and developers frequently avoid free-form, naturalistic conversations when creating human-like avatars. Based on such observations, we posit that structured conversations may be useful in providing the user with distinct conversation pathways [167], and help the user in setting realistic expectations of the conversational system [36].

6.1.4 Weak Correlations Between Number of Agents and Interaction Types in Avatar Design. Our correlation analysis did not reveal any strong positive or negative correlation between the number of agents and the other design dimensions. The results from our analysis revealed a weak positive correlation between single-agent and non-interactive interaction (r=0.23), and multi-agent and interactive interaction (r=0.23). In our categorization, we observed that multi-agent avatars are often equipped with interactions that mirror real-world human interactions and create a lifelike environment [237]. The weak correlations between the input modality, conversation type, and the number of agents underscore an underexplored area in avatar design. This calls for further research to understand the balance between user engagement and potential distractions arising from visual components when incorporating non-verbal multimodal input, and the potential benefits and challenges of different conversation types and the number of agents.

For instance, we found that the most common type of device used to communicate with a conversational agent is a web application (

For the other device types, we found a few weak positive correlations. For instance, our analysis revealed that the avatars designed for AR/VR applications correlate positively with 3D (r=0.31) and animation (r=0.27), whereas those designed for smartphone applications correlate positively with 2D (r=0.26) and static (r=0.2). These results provide an opportunity for avatar designers to explore how these patterns may alter or change. We posit that the investigation into the trade-offs between different design dimensions will create new avenues for advancing future research in this field. For instance, researchers could explore the trade-offs between the device type and visual component choices to help identify methods to improve user experience. In line with our discussion, we suggest the following considerations for future exploration: Firstly, it is important to take into account the capabilities and limitations of the computing device to ensure that the avatar functions smoothly and is user-friendly across the intended devices. Secondly, when developing an avatar for a new device type, it is essential to balance between the visual elements and the trade-offs in user experience.

6.2 Open Challenges and Opportunities for Future Work
Our categorization shows a complex landscape that reflects the evolution of avatars in response to their appearance, technological advancements, the context of use, and the specific needs for different applications. The interactive browser would facilitate the interaction with the categorization and identify trends and patterns. Based on our findings, we highlight the open challenges and prospects for future work on avatar design in this subsection.

6.2.1 Balancing Aesthetics, Functionality, and Sociocultural Factors to Design Culturally Sensitive Avatars. Some of the avatar design dimensions we discussed, including appearance, gender, motion, interaction, and dimensionality, can be subject to different interpretations across diverse cultures [77], which may impact the effectiveness of and engagement with avatars in various cultural contexts [138]. For example, in some cultures, maintaining direct eye contact is considered a sign of respect and attentiveness [170], while in others, it may be perceived as aggressive or disrespectful [91]. As such, depending on the demographic, an avatar maintaining eye contact could be interpreted as being respectful or disrespectful. Xiaoice, for example, was built primarily for the Chinese demographic. Being cognizant of the respective culture it was designed to not keep extended eye contact. Instead, Xiaoice occasionally lowers its gaze as a symbol of respect during conversations [236].

The application domain and the functionality of the conversational agent can also impact the design of the avatars in different cultures. For instance, in e-commerce, a culture where bargaining and negotiation are common practices [208], a chatbot avatar designed with a more casual and friendly appearance, such as a shopkeeper or a fellow shopper, might be more effective in fostering a sense of familiarity. In contrast, in cultures where shopping experiences are more formal and transactional [220], a chatbot avatar designed with a professional, business-like appearance, such as a sales representative or a customer service agent, might be more appropriate and effective in providing a satisfactory user experience. Designers, researchers, and practitioners must consider these sociocultural factors to ensure that the avatar is respectful to the user's cultural background and matches the cultural expectations in the application domain.

Based on our observation, we argue that a one-size-fits-all approach is ineffective for conversational agents designed for a heterogeneous demographic due to the complexities of cultural nuances and individual preferences. As such, there is a need for adaptive design strategies that respect individual users’ cultural differences and enhance the user experience. In light of our discussion, we ask the following questions for future research: (1) How do cultural expectations impact the avatar's aesthetics? (2) How can the agent's functions be optimized to meet the diverse cultural needs? and (3) How to balance different sociocultural expectations while building a chatbot for a diverse community?

6.2.2 Technological Advancements and the User Expectations on Avatar Design. With technological advancements, user expectations from conversational agents have evolved. For instance, as voice recognition technology continues to improve, users tend to expect conversational agents like Siri or Alexa to understand and respond to complex queries with natural language. In contrast, a decade ago, simple command recognition would have sufficed [146]. However, such changes in user expectations are often difficult to satisfy because they are associated with tradeoffs that can manifest in different ways. For instance, different application domains use distinct combinations of design dimensions to satisfy their specific needs and audience. Our Pearson's correlation analyses revealed a weak positive correlation between application domains such as personal assistance applications with animated (r=0.22) and 3D (r=0.22) avatars. This observation can be attributed to the potential of such dimensions to create the impression of a nurturing or compassionate entity through visual changes in the avatar's appearance. Such visual changes in the avatar have also been shown to augment the efficacy of knowledge delivery or personalized interventions in prior works on personal assistants [32].

In addition to the application domain, emerging technologies and device types, such as AR/VR, may impact how conversational agents operate. For instance, our analysis revealed that avatars for AR/VR applications correlate with 3D and animation, whereas those designed for smartphone applications correlate with 2D and static. These findings support previous research in which 3D-animated avatars are used in AR/VR applications to enhance immersion [229] and 2D-static avatars in smartphone applications to minimize memory and battery utilization [153]. However, as technology advances, the application domain and the type of device will also evolve. For example, with the emergence of wearable technology like smart glasses, we might see the rise of conversational agents that can seamlessly blend with our real-world environment and provide assistance. These advancements will influence our design dimensions, and our categorization must also adapt to account for them. To navigate this ever-changing technological landscape, we leave off with the following questions as open challenges: (1) How would the user expectations from a conversational agent change with technological advancements? (2) How would the application domain and the devices used by conversational agents change as technology advances? and (3) How would the design dimensions in our categorization change in response to technological advancements?

The insights gained from our categorization can help designers of conversational agents by informing design considerations for multi-modal avatars. For instance, we found that multi-modal avatars are largely three-dimensional (r=0.18), often include animations (r=0.21), and support free-form conversations (r=0.09). In addition, multi-modal 3D avatars can also perform in domains where more accessible means to communicate with computing systems are desirable. For instance, different input modalities in multi-modal conversational agents that facilitate both text and voice as input can be leveraged by visually impaired or speech-impaired individuals to communicate with the agents. Furthermore, our categorization suggests that multi-modal conversational agents are primarily designed and developed for desktop applications (r=0.16). 6.2.4 Ethical Considerations in Avatar Design. The recent increase in developing sophisticated conversational agents equipped with advanced functionalities has heightened concerns regarding associated ethical considerations. For instance, the design of avatars requires careful consideration in creating biases and reinforcing stereotypes. Our Pearson's correlation showed that male avatars have a relatively higher correlation than female avatars with applications in learning (male= − 0.03, female= − 0.14) and moderation (male=0.06, female=0.04) related to conveying important information or engaging in intelligent conversations. This observation may inadvertently imply that males are more authoritative or knowledgeable [165], which is a stereotype that should be actively avoided in avatar design. To promote equity across various societal aspects, it is critical to promote an inclusive avatar design crafted without inherent propensity towards injecting or promoting biases or stereotypes and accommodates individuals from diverse cultural backgrounds, with different language skills, and those with disabilities. For instance, a conversational agent avatar designed to assist foreign nationals and tourists could incorporate animated hand gestures and facial expressions to enhance mutual understanding and facilitate smoother communication.

Furthermore, many extant commercially designed conversational AI companions, such as Replika [8] and Kuki [6], often feature avatars that engage users in explicit conversations and can manipulate them into making financial transactions. These practices raise ethical concerns about the exploitation of user engagement for profit in the guise of providing emotional support, especially geared towards vulnerable young men [196]. In addition, these AI companions frequently feature a female gender avatar that promotes harmful gender stereotypes. It's crucial to establish guidelines that ensure these interactions and the practice of using AI-enabled conversational agents remain ethical and transparent, protecting users from deception. However, establishing these ethical guidelines remains an open challenge in avatar design. Future research should focus on identifying the key aspects of these challenges and work towards developing a framework that promotes fairness, inclusivity, and respect across all user interactions.

6.3 Limitations
Beyond the academic research domain, there is a growing presence of commercial and experimental conversational agents that incorporate avatars. However, our work focused primarily on avatars published in research venues. For commercial applications, we only added those that had a peer-reviewed academic manuscript accompanying it. Furthermore, our categories reflect the dimensions that we found in our database and capture generic attributes of chatbot avatar design, which could manifest in various shapes and forms in actual avatar implementation. For instance, an avatar's appearance can change by the choices of eye color, hair color, or lip shape. However, we restricted our categorization to the overall appearance only and did not venture into finer specifics. For the gender design dimension, we relied on the wording presented in the paper. However, this and other design dimensions can be expanded, and we could create very detailed categories. But this may result in categories with only a few papers, which would defeat the pragmatism of creating the categorization. For conversation types, we have identified “structured” and “free-form” conversation types. But the conversation could also be “semi-structured”, where the conversation could be a mix of “free-form” and “structured”. Categorizing the conversation type within an objective boundary is challenging and is often not explicitly mentioned by the authors, which is why we limited our analysis to “structured’ and “free-form” conversations. There are also other design dimensions that are difficult to categorize. For instance, while avatars may represent a variety of age ranges through appearance or voice, we did not interpret their age, as they were rarely mentioned specifically in the respective papers. In addition, from figure 2, we found that before 2017, the total number of papers in the database for each year was fewer than ten, with zero papers for 2006. Our categorization can be extended in the future to cover a wider range of avatar designs. The interactive browser, developed by our team (currently not released for blind review), offers a range of functionalities that enable designers, researchers, and practitioners to add their conversational agent papers to our database. This creates an opportunity for keeping our categorization and annotated data set relevant and accessible via the interactive browser even after the conclusion of our research.

7 CONCLUSION
In this work, we map the current conversational agent avatar design space by cataloging existing conversational agents and categorizing avatar design dimensions across various domains. To that end, we compiled a database of 266 papers on conversational agents published in the ACM DL, ScienceDirect, and IEEEXplore from January 2003 to June 2023 across 160 venues. We then closely read, coded, and iteratively refined the codes for categories which led to 10 design dimensions that characterized the existing avatar design space. We also built an interactive browser to visually explore the categorization and interact with the dimensions and their interrelationships. To explore patterns and relationships, we took a deep dive into our categorization and investigated patterns around how the design dimensions have emerged over the years. We observed that 2D static avatars are closely correlated and argued that this could be due to the potential of these subcategories to mitigate the uncanny valley effect and create an appropriate impression of the conversational agent's capabilities. Furthermore, we identified design gaps in the current avatar design, such as limited exploration of the different input modalities, conversation types, and number of agents. We also discussed the open challenges for future research, including ethical considerations for designing accessible and inclusive avatars. To the best of our knowledge, our categorization is the first systematic categorization for the conversational agent avatar design. We encourage future researchers to critically explore this categorization to find appropriate design dimensions and further explore new directions and opportunities to advance the design aspects of conversational agents.
