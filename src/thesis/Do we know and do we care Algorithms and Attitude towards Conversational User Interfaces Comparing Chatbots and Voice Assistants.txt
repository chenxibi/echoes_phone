Do we know and do we care? Algorithms and Attitude towards Conversational User Interfaces: Comparing Chatbots and Voice Assistants
Sara Irma Parnell, University of Tübingen, Germany, sar.parnell@googlemail.com
Stefanie Helene Klein, Everyday Media Lab, Leibniz-Institut für Wissensmedien, Germany, s.klein@iwm-tuebingen.de
Franziska Gaiser, Everyday Media Lab, Leibniz-Institut für Wissensmedien, Germany, f.gaiser@iwm-tuebingen.de

DOI: https://doi-org.aalto-libproxy.idm.oclc.org/10.1145/3543829.3544517
CUI 2022: 4th Conference on Conversational User Interfaces, Glasgow, United Kingdom, July 2022

As conversational user interfaces (CUIs) are increasingly integrated into daily life, ethical and societal concerns about integrated content filtering algorithms emerge. In addressing these concerns, it is essential to know how aware and knowledgeable society is of the algorithms it encounters using CUIs and the extent to which this impacts the attitude towards these technologies. In this survey study, we made a first attempt to measure and compare participants’ algorithm awareness of chatbots and voice assistants. Further, we assessed the effect of algorithm literacy on the attitude towards CUIs and possible interaction effects with technology acceptance. Lastly, we compared previous and future usage purposes for chatbots and voice assistants. We found higher algorithm awareness for voice assistants than for chatbots. No correlation between algorithm literacy and attitude towards either chatbots or voice assistants was found. An additional personal-level factor, technology acceptance, did not affect this relationship. The results show that participants preferred using voice assistants for task completion and social purposes over chatbots, while getting information was equally preferred between chatbots and voice assistants. Considering its societal relevance, we want to encourage more research on algorithmic awareness and understanding in the field of CUI and its cognitive and behavioral effects.

1 INTRODUCTION
Conversational user interfaces (CUIs), which users can talk to in real-time, without human activity needed [19], are becoming increasingly part of everyday life. “A conversational agent consists of a software program that interacts with a user by interpreting and responding to user statements in natural language” [42]. These agents can be text-based chatbots, such as service chatbots, to assist customers via text dialogues in their online shopping experience. Similarly, social chatbots, such as Replika or Kuki can help fulfill needs for “communication, affection, and social belonging” [38]. Voice-based CUIs, called voice assistants, such as Amazon Alexa or Siri, in contrast, can answer general questions, control the smart home or play media via speech commands [17].

Chatbots and voice assistants are getting increasingly popular. As of 2018, 90% of Americans have heard of voice assistants before, of which 72% have already used one [29]. Approximately two-thirds of consumers worldwide reported interacting with a chatbot in the past twelve months [37].

These CUIs have in common that communication with them, whether text-based or voice-based, is supported by algorithms that are able to select or generate the most appropriate answer to a user's input [12]. Chatbots will respond to specific keywords and find the optimal answer based on previous conversations, but responses might be biased or include prejudice [1, 5].

Voice assistants, such as Google Home or Amazon Echo, also use additional personal information to recommend products or media content based on the user's preferences; however, they often do not present multiple answers to choose from, making users even more dependent on the algorithms behind the search [15, 20].

Especially when interacting with CUIs, which are becoming more and more human-like and able to make autonomous decisions, users’ everyday freedom of choice may be more easily threatened – impacting not only individuals but society as a whole [35]. Being aware of and understanding the role of algorithms could enable users to critically evaluate the content presented to them by voice assistants and chatbots [11].

However, neither people's awareness and understanding of these algorithms nor their impact on their attitude toward CUIs has been studied a lot [45]. Specifically, algorithm awareness can be defined as “the extent to which people hold accurate perceptions of what algorithms do in a particular media environment, as well as their impact on how users consume and experience media content” [45]. So far, research on algorithm awareness is mainly confined to (social) media platforms, like Facebook, YouTube, or other online contexts [8, 9, 27, 31, 39], but neglected voice assistants and chatbots, even though the topic might be especially relevant here.

Therefore, the primary goal of this study is to understand how aware users are of (content filtering) algorithms in CUIs by comparing chatbots and voice assistants. Moreover, we analyze whether users’ understanding of algorithms affects their attitude towards CUIs. Additionally, we look at self-reported previous and future usage purposes of chatbots and voice assistants. With this survey study, we make a first attempt to understand the differences in the type of usage and attitude towards chatbots and voice assistants and, more importantly, the role of algorithms in that.

2 RESEARCH QUESTIONS AND HYPOTHESES
2.1 Algorithm Awareness and Literacy
Research on algorithm awareness of CUIs is scarce. As a recent exception, Gruber et al. [2021] conducted a first qualitative study on algorithm awareness in voice assistants and found a large variety among users. Participants who were aware of algorithms to some degree mainly based this on their experiences with social media or other online platforms rather than their own experience with voice assistants. This finding is not surprising, as most suppliers of voice assistants are big data companies, which are known for their highly developed algorithmic content curation [7, 16]. This is not the case with chatbots, which are being used in a plethora of companies, such as travel [22], finance [30], and health care [25]. Hence, we predict higher awareness for content filtering algorithms in voice assistants than in chatbots:

H1: Algorithmic media content awareness is higher for voice assistants than for chatbots.

Additionally, we explore the relationship between algorithm literacy and attitude towards CUIs. The concept of algorithm literacy goes beyond algorithm awareness. More specifically, people high in literacy are not only aware but also knowledgeable about algorithms they encounter online and can evaluate and cope with them effectively [10]. Studies have found that one aspect of algorithm literacy, namely knowledge about algorithms, positively relates to perceptions of algorithm-generated recommendations and attitudes towards them. For instance, subjects’ perceptions of news recommendations were predicted by their degree of self-reported knowledge about AI, algorithms, and computer programming [2].

Furthermore, Yeomans et al. [2019] showed that algorithm-generated recommendations are preferred when participants understand how the algorithm works. Algorithm literacy has been considered an important skill for using conversational agents effectively. Individuals with higher algorithmic skills may be better at understanding and using voice assistants [15]. Assuming that people with higher algorithm literacy have a better evaluation of the algorithmic output and better skills in using conversational AI, we believe that they also have a more positive attitude towards chatbots and voice assistants:

H2: Algorithm literacy and attitude towards chatbots and voice assistants are positively correlated.

Still, the relationship between algorithm literacy and attitude may be more complex than a simple linear correlation [27]. Indeed, Gran et al. [2021] found increased negative and positive attitudes with higher awareness of algorithmic recommendations. Higher algorithm literacy may lead to increased negative and positive attitudes, depending on the interaction with an additional variable.

Therefore, we also look at an additional personality-related factor, technology acceptance, to see if and how this intertwines with the previous assumptions. Technology acceptance refers to a person's attitude towards technological progress. In particular, it relates to an individual's personal relationship to modern technologies and not their evaluation in terms of their significance for society [6, 26]. People high in this trait have a strong personal interest in technological innovations [26]. Previous research has linked people's degree of technology acceptance to attitudes towards robots for smart home usage [3] and education [32], as well as risk and trust perceptions around smart homes [24]. Therefore, we are interested to see if there is an interaction effect between algorithm literacy and technology acceptance on the attitude towards chatbots or voice assistants. The degree of technology acceptance will possibly alter the strength of the relationship between algorithm literacy and attitude towards AI. This relationship may also differ between chatbots and voice assistants:

RQ1: Is there an interaction between technology acceptance, algorithm literacy, and the type of conversational agent on the attitude towards conversational AI?

2.2 Previous and Future Usage
Considering that CUIs will further develop and improve [28, 41], we are also interested to find out what people are currently using and can imagine using this technology for in the future and whether this differs for the two types of agents. Following the uses and gratification approach, people choose and use specific media and technologies to fulfill certain social and psychological needs [4, 33]. Therefore, users also actively choose to rely on chatbots or voice assistants for specific needs or gratification. We are interested to see if these needs vary between conversational interfaces by comparing the ways the agents are used.

One of the most common use purposes for CUIs is requesting information [4, 42]. In voice assistants, these usually consist of quick informational searches [23], while chatbots typically inform their users mainly in the context of customer service [4]. Secondly, CUIs can complete simple tasks. Chatbots are, e.g., used to complete an order [4], whereas voice assistants serve as smart home control or media players [17, 23, 42]. Besides these common uses, CUIs can also be used to fulfill emotional and social needs [4, 21]. Surprisingly, Xu et al. [2017] found that about 40% of customer service chatbot requests are rather emotional-based than search-based. Indeed, interest is growing in CUIs that go beyond performance on specific tasks but are also capable of having authentic conversations [13]. Since there is no direct comparison yet between chatbots and voice assistants, we decided to look at three use purposes that apply to both chatbots and voice assistants. This includes (1) completing tasks, (2) getting information, and (3) social interaction. With this, we aim to understand better what use purposes people prefer and if those differ between chatbots and voice assistants.

RQ2: What have people used conversational AI for, and what may they want to use it for in the future? How does this differ between chatbots and voice assistants?

3 METHOD
3.1 Sample and Procedure
We conducted an online survey and recruited participants via the online sampling platform prolific. Participants had to be at least 18 years old, UK citizens, and have English as their first language. After participation, subjects received compensation. Prior experience with chatbots or voice assistants was not required. We preregistered the study before having access to the data (https://aspredicted.org/Y7Q_PW2).

We recruited1 a total of 409 participants. Among the participants, 208 (50.86%) were females and 199 (48.66%) males, with an average age of 42 years (SD = 14.30, range 18 - 80 years). Most subjects (N = 317, 77.51%) had studied or were currently studying at a higher education institution. A similar number of subjects had used a chatbot (62.59%) or a voice assistant (67.73%) before.

3.2 Measures
Algorithm awareness was measured with the algorithmic media content awareness (AMCA) scale by Zarouali et al. [2021]. The items were adapted for chatbots and voice assistants each (e.g., “Voice assistants/ Chatbots use algorithms to recommend information to me.”) and ranged from 1 (not at all aware) to 5 (completely aware). Four items were removed because their statements did not apply to either chatbots or voice assistants. Thereby, we avoided asking for people's awareness of an incorrect statement.

Algorithm literacy was measured with the algorithm literacy scale by Dogruel et al. [2021], containing 23 algorithm-related knowledge questions for participants to answer (e.g., “I can influence algorithms with my internet usage behavior.”).

Attitude toward chatbots and voice assistants was measured using three adapted items of Hsu and Lin [2016] (e.g., “I like using voice assistants/ chatbots”). The scale ranged from 1 (not agree at all) to 7 (fully agree).

Technology acceptance items were extracted from the subscale of the technology commitment scale by Neyer et al. [2012] and were translated from German (e.g., “Regarding new technical developments, I am very curious.”). The items were measured on a scale ranging from 1 (not agree at all) to 7 (fully agree).

Specific usage intention was measured with two questions: (1) When using chatbots/voice assistants in the past, what did you use them for?’ (2) ‘In the future, when chatbots/voice assistants may be more developed: For which purpose could you imagine using chatbots/ voice assistants?’. We presented separate questions for chatbots and voice assistants. Answer options were a. Getting information [23, 42], b. Completing tasks [4, 42], c. ‘Social’ Interaction [4, 42, 23], and d. Other. Multiple options could be selected.

4 RESULTS
4.1 Algorithm Awareness, Algorithm Literacy, and Attitude
Results showed that subjects have significantly higher algorithmic media content awareness for voice assistants (M = 47.25, SD = 12.60), than for chatbots (M = 45.88, SD = 12.66; t(408) = –3.59, p < 0.001). The mean difference between conversational AI media was small (η2 = .02). Therefore, H1 could be confirmed.

Correlational analysis revealed that algorithm literacy scores were not correlated with attitude (r(407) = .02, p = .607). Therefore, we could not confirm H2.

We computed a linear mixed-effect model to analyze the possible three-way interaction between algorithm literacy, technology acceptance, and the type of CUI on attitude ratings. An exploratory correlation analysis and a paired t-test performed beforehand yielded a positive correlation between technology acceptance and attitude (r(407) = .45, p < .001) and higher attitude ratings for voice assistants (M = 12.44, SD = 5.57) compared to chatbots (M = 8.72, SD = 5.21; t(408) = 13.62, p < .001, η2 = 0.67). The comparison between a model excluding the three-way interaction and a second model including the three-way interaction showed no significant difference (χ2 = 0.003, p = .954). Both models were corrected for age and gender. Algorithm literacy scores, technology acceptance scores, and the conversational AI medium did not interact in a unique way to affect attitude ratings towards the medium (see Figure 1).

However, for exploratory purposes, we looked at the two-way interaction between technology acceptance and CUI type, and we found a significant effect: The positive relation between technology acceptance and attitude was stronger for voice assistants than for chatbots. The difference between a model including the interaction and a model excluding the interaction was significant (χ2 = 11.63, p = <.001). Both models were corrected for age and gender.

4.2 Previous and Future Usage
To compare previous and future usage between the different conversational agents, we performed six separate McNemar‘s ChiSquare tests. A higher share of participants indicated having used voice assistants (vs. chatbots) for completing tasks (χ2(1, N = 409) = 63.44, p < .001), and for social interaction (χ2(1, N = 409) = 46.30, p < .001). Regarding information acquisition, we did not find a difference between the types of CUI (χ2(1, N = 409) = 0.88, p = .349). A similar pattern for future use purposes emerged: Participants indicated using voice assistants rather than chatbots for task completion (χ2(1, N = 409) = 42.47, p < .001) and social interaction (χ2(1, N = 409) = 16.36, p <.001). No difference was found for getting information (χ2(1, N = 409) = 0.13, p = 0.718). For visualizations see Figures 2 and 3.

5 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
In this survey study, we investigated algorithm awareness, literacy, and usage fields concerning chatbots and voice assistants to get first insights and better understand the difference between these CUIs. As predicted, algorithmic awareness was higher for voice assistants than for chatbots. This indicates that the usage of algorithms is more present for users in voice assistants which could be due to a higher association with big data companies compared to chatbots.

Concerning our second hypothesis, attitude was not affected by algorithm literacy; neither for chatbots nor voice assistants. This does not support previous findings on positive effects of algorithm knowledge on users’ perceptions of conversational agents [2, 44]. Though algorithm literacy may not positively affect the users’ attitudes towards chatbots or voice assistants, this may still have important implications for CUI providers. As CUI companies are hardly transparent to the public concerning their algorithms, they might be concerned that revealing these would negatively affect users’ perceptions of the technology. However, the present findings suggest this may not be the case. Knowing this could increase the likelihood of CUI companies being more transparent with their algorithms.

Additionally, as asked about in RQ1, we did not find a significant three-way interaction between technology acceptance, algorithm literacy, and the type of CUI on attitude. However, since research on the relationship between algorithm literacy and attitude is sparse [27], more studies with conversational agents should be conducted before making conclusions. Most likely, the relationship is more complex than a simple linear correlation [27] or may interact with other factors than technology acceptance. Future studies may look for interaction effects between algorithm literacy and other technological factors on attitude, such as privacy concerns and trust [34, 36, 40].

Lastly, in RQ2, we were interested in previous and future use of the agents. Results showed similar patterns for past usage and future usage intentions for both CUIs. Results, however, also indicate that users choose chatbots and voice assistants to fulfill certain needs to varying degrees [4, 33]. Generally, results suggest that voice assistants fulfill the needs for social interaction and task completion more than chatbots, while the need for getting information is similarly fulfilled in both agents. Since the current study is one of the first to compare the frequency of use purposes chosen for chatbots and voice assistants, it is hard to interpret the reasons for the differences found without further research. One possible explanation could be that users might still associate chatbots with specific tasks and transactions, whereas one voice assistant can perform multiple tasks and operate on a more human-like modality (voice).

There are several limitations to consider. Since this was a pilot study with a cross-sectional design, we cannot draw conclusions regarding causality direction. To tackle this problem, experimental or longitudinal designs should be used. Additionally, we used response options for previous and future use that have not been psychometrically validated and do not aspire to comprehensiveness and reliability standards. Despite this, similar future and previous usage patterns and larger response percentages for future compared to previous usage indicate that the questions were correctly understood. Future research may aim to collect theory-based and validated use purposes for CUI that can be tested across CUI research.

This study provides a starting point for measuring algorithm awareness and understanding how algorithm literacy may affect attitudes towards CUIs. Hence, findings are tentative, and conclusions cannot be made without further research into this topic. Considering its societal relevance, we want to encourage more research on algorithmic awareness and understanding in the field of CUI and its cognitive and behavioral consequences.
