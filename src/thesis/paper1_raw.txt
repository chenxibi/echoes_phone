Abstract 
This thesis studies the impact of designing an emotionally intelligent home -based 
reminiscence therapy (RT) system on older adults’ user engagement and experience 
when interacting with an AI conversational assistant, as well as its effects on 
supporting caregivers in RT collaboration. The motivation  comes from the 
increasing psychological and emotional challenges faced by the aging population. 
While RT is a well -established psychosocial intervention to enhance emotional 
well-being and identity continuity, its application at home without professional 
facilitation remains limited by the lack of emotional guidance  and tools f or 
monitoring emotional responses. 
 
The research follows a user-centered design (UCD) framework. Secondary research 
was conducted during the context of use analysis to explore the challenges of home-
based RT and to identify the needs of older adults and caregivers. The study then 
proceeded with persona developm ent, task analysis, iterative prototyping, and 
remote usability testing involving older adult-caregiver pairs. 
 
The main results and findings indicate that the designed high -fidelity prototype, 
which combines a web-based voice user interface with real-time emotion detection 
and a caregiver dashboard for emotional insights, was well accepted by users. Older 
adults de scribed the system as intuitive and emotionally supportive, while 
caregivers appreciated the emotional summaries for monitoring well -being and 
tailoring future sessions. The evaluation also revealed improvement opportunities, 
including enhancing content pe rsonalization, clarifying emotional scoring, and 
refining speech interaction cues.  This thesis contributes practical design insights 
for developing empathetic, transparent, and accessible digital mental health tools. 
The findings demonstrate the potential of emotion -aware voice-based systems to 
complement human care and facilitate meaningful emotional experiences for older 
adults in home settings. 
Keywords  reminiscence therapy, voice user interface, emotional intelligence, 
aging in place

1 Introduction
This chapter introduces the topic of the thesis, explains the motivation for conducting
the project, provides the context and background, defines the main objectives, and
outlines the structure of the document.
1.1 Motivation
As the global population grows older, more older adults face mental health chal-
lenges such as loneliness, depression, and cognitive decline [1, 2]. These demographic
changes highlight the urgent need for supportive approaches that not only address
medical conditions but also help maintain emotional well-being and social connected-
ness [3].
Reminiscence therapy (RT) has emerged as a well-established psychosocial interven-
tion that encourages older adults to revisit, reflect on, and share meaningful mem-
ories. Research consistently shows that RT can reduce depressive symptoms and
increase life satisfaction among older adults, both with and without cognitive impair-
ment [4, 5, 6]. Systematic reviews confirm that well-designed RT programmes deliver
significant psychological benefits [4, 5].
However, reminiscence does not always lead to positive outcomes. Certain forms of
reminiscence, such as obsessive or bitterness-focused reflection, can evoke negative
emotions like sadness or regret [7, 8]. Therefore, it is important to design tools that
help older adults shift towards self-positive and integrative forms of reminiscence,
which have been associated with greater psychological well-being [9, 8].
In parallel, many societies increasingly promote ageing-in-place, encouraging older
adults to remain in their own homes for as long as possible. Informal caregivers, such
as family members or in-home support workers, often facilitate home-based reminis-
cence sessions. While promising, these sessions are emotionally complex and time-
intensive, and there is often no systematic way to monitor their emotional impact or
effectiveness [1, 6].
Recent advances in voice user interfaces (VUIs) and emotion-aware artificial intelli-
gence create opportunities to address these challenges. By combining natural, hands-
free voice interaction with real-time emotional awareness, it is possible to design sys-
tems that support older adults in engaging, guided reminiscence and provide useful
emotional insights for caregivers [2, 9]. This thesis explores how such a system can be
designed and what role it can play. The goal is not to replace therapists, but to aug-
ment human care with technology that supports mental well-being and meaningful
connection.
In summary, the ageing population faces growing mental health challenges that can-
not be addressed by medical treatment alone. Reminiscence therapy has proven po-
tential to improve older adults’ emotional well-being, yet its effectiveness depends
on carefully guiding the experience to avoid negative emotional outcomes. Emerg-
ing voice-based and emotion-aware technologies offer promising ways to make home-

based reminiscence more engaging and supportive for both older adults and their
informal caregivers. To further clarify this need and motivation, the next section pro-
vides additional context and background for the study.
1.2 Context
Reminiscence therapy has been studied for decades, dating back to Butler’s early
work on life review [7]. Since then, it has evolved into structured and unstructured
formats, used in both clinical and home settings. Meta-analyses have demonstrated
its effectiveness for a range of target groups: people with dementia [6], cognitively
healthy older adults [4, 5], and those facing life transitions or loss [7].
Meanwhile, studies show that digital tools and AI-based technologies can help support
older adults’ cognitive and emotional health [3]. Voice-based systems align well with
the needs of older adults because they require minimal technical skills and allow for
natural interaction [2, 1]. Emotion-aware AI has the potential to adapt conversations
in real time, supporting older adults to stay engaged and avoid negative emotional
spirals [9, 8].
Despite this potential, existing digital RT tools mostly focus on providing simple prompts
like photos or music and rarely include emotion-aware adaptation or systematic feed-
back for caregivers [6].
In sum, this project builds on knowledge from psychology, ageing research, and
human-computer interaction (HCI) to address this gap by designing and evaluat-
ing a prototype that integrates a voice-based conversational interface with emotion-
awareness for home use. Based on this context, the main objectives of this thesis are
described in the next section.
1.3 Objectives
The main aim of this thesis is to investigate how an emotionally intelligent, voice-based
conversational system can support home-based reminiscence therapy for older adults
while providing informal caregivers with meaningful emotional insights. Specifically,
the goals can be divided into tasks below:
• Design and develop a prototype that combines a voice interface with emotion
detection features suitable for older adults and their caregivers.
• Investigate how emotion-aware voice interaction affects how older adults feel and
engage during reminiscence sessions.
• Test how well the system can provide useful feedback to caregivers by summaris-
ing emotional trends and showing which prompts encourage positive responses
and higher engagement.

2 State of the Art
This chapter reviews the current state of the art related to this work, explains the main
ideas and previous studies on reminiscence therapy, describes how voice user inter-
faces and emotion-aware AI have been used to support older adults, and highlights
the research gaps that guide the aims and design of this thesis.
2.1 Reminiscence Therapy
Reminiscence therapy (RT) is a psychosocial intervention designed to encourage indi-
viduals, particularly older adults, to recall, reflect on, and share significant personal
memories. The concept was originally introduced by Butler in 1963, who argued that
reviewing one’s life supports identity continuity and promotes psychological well-being
in later life [7]. RT sessions can be delivered individually or in groups, typically us-
ing tangible prompts such as photographs, music, personal objects, or storytelling to
evoke autobiographical memories [6].
Different types of RT have been described based on their psychological functions.
Commonly recognised forms include integrative, instrumental, spiritual, and trans-
missive reminiscence, each serving unique roles such as maintaining identity, resolv-
ing conflicts, or transmitting life lessons [8, 9]. The functions of reminiscence can
be broadly categorised by their focus (self-focused or socially focused) and typical
emotional outcomes (positive or negative) [8]. For example, integrative and instrumen-
tal reminiscence are generally associated with positive psychological benefits, while
obsessive or escapist reminiscence can evoke unresolved negative emotions such as
sadness or guilt [8].
Empirical studies consistently demonstrate RT’s effectiveness in improving older adults’
psychological and social well-being. For example, Xu et al. [4] found that RT sig-
nificantly reduces depressive symptoms and enhances life satisfaction among older
adults without obvious cognitive impairment. Tam et al. [5] reported similar positive
effects, including improved self-esteem and overall psychological well-being for cogni-
tively healthy older adults. For individuals living with dementia, systematic reviews
have shown that RT can lead to modest improvements in mood, communication, and
cognitive function [6].
However, research also highlights that RT’s effects are not always uniformly positive.
Certain types of reminiscence, such as obsessive rumination or bitterness revival, can
amplify negative feelings if not properly guided [7, 8]. Figure 2.1 illustrates how dif-
ferent reminiscence types are typically grouped by their emotional valence and focus,
based on the work of Cappeliez, Guindon, and Robitaille [8].
Despite its proven benefits, many current RT implementations lack real-time sensitiv-
ity to participants’ emotional states. In practice, sessions often follow a fixed format
and rely on static prompts without adapting to users’ emotional responses or shifting
needs [4]. This “one-size-fits-all” approach can sometimes result in disengagement
or even unintended distress [5]. Few systems incorporate emotional monitoring or
adaptive content delivery that could tailor sessions to each individual.

This research responds to this gap by proposing an emotionally adaptive, voice-based
conversational system for home-based reminiscence therapy. By detecting emotional
cues in real time and adjusting prompts as needed, the system aims to support more
positive engagement and reduce the risk of negative feelings. It also gives informal
caregivers clear emotional summaries and shows which prompts work best, helping
them plan future sessions more effectively and strengthen their support role. To un-
derstand why voice-based interaction is especially suited for this purpose, the next
section explains how voice user interfaces can help older adults benefit from emotion-
ally sensitive digital support.
2.2 Voice User Interfaces for Older Adults
Voice User Interfaces (VUIs) let users interact with a system through spoken lan-
guage, giving hands-free and natural access to digital tools. For older adults, this
form of interaction matches their lifelong habit of talking and helps overcome age-
related challenges such as declining vision, limited dexterity, or lower digital skills
[10, 11]. Because of these qualities, VUIs can play an important role in delivering
more emotionally sensitive and accessible reminiscence experiences at home.
5
--- Page 12 ---
Recent literature validates several key benefits of VUIs for older adults. Firstly, VUIs
reduce cognitive and physical effort, allowing intuitive interaction without the need
for screens or keyboards [10]. Secondly, Voice agents can create a sense of compan-
ionship, meeting emotional needs and helping reduce loneliness among older adults
[11, 12]. Thirdly, VUIs can support medication reminders, daily routines, and emer-
gency communication, thus helping older adults maintain independence at home [12].
Lastly, conversational systems have been used for telehealth, memory training, and
digital literacy interventions, demonstrating promising applications for healthy ageing
[11, 12].
Despite these advantages, challenges persist. Studies show that older adults often
prefer voice systems that feel more human-like, empathetic, and attentive. Those
qualities still limited in many current VUIs [11]. Additional concerns include speech
recognition errors, lack of transparency, and information accuracy, especially when
discussing sensitive or emotional topics [10, 12]. Rigid conversational scripts and
delayed system responses can lead to frustration, particularly for users with hearing
or cognitive limitations.
These insights suggest clear design directions for the development of emotion-aware
VUIs in reminiscence therapy:
• Emotion-Adaptive Dialogue: Systems should detect stress, confusion, or posi-
tive feelings through real-time voice cues and adjust prompts or tone in response.
• Narrative Structuring: Reminiscence prompts designed as short, personal sto-
ries can deepen emotional connection and support memory recall.
• Supportive Presence: VUIs should create a sense of emotional safety during
sensitive sessions by using a caring voice tone, clear explanations, and friendly
feedback.
Building on these insights, the following section discusses how advances in emotion-
ally intelligent AI can further enhance VUI interactions for reminiscence therapy.
2.3 AI and Emotional Intelligence
Recent advances in affective computing and natural language processing have en-
abled the development of emotionally intelligent AI systems capable of recognising,
interpreting, and responding to human affect [13]. Such systems are increasingly
used to address loneliness, foster engagement, and support mental well-being in older
adult populations [14].
For example, in a pilot study, Abdollahi et al. [15] demonstrated that a socially as-
sistive robot equipped with artificial emotional intelligence significantly improved user
trust and satisfaction by tailoring its verbal and nonverbal responses to detected emo-
tional cues. Likewise, a systematic review by Yang et al. [14] showed that AI-enabled
interventions that adapt to users’ emotions are more effective at fostering companion-
ship and reducing loneliness than non-adaptive systems.
Within the context of reminiscence therapy, emotionally intelligent AI can play a trans-
formative role. While conventional digital tools rely on static prompts, affect-aware
6
--- Page 13 ---
agents can dynamically adjust tone, pacing, or conversational content in response
to a user’s emotional state, enhancing personalisation and reducing disengagement
risks [13]. Supporting this, Delhom, Satorres, and Meléndez [16] demonstrated that
emotional intelligence training can help older adults improve their mood and coping
strategies, highlighting the value of integrating such capabilities into AI systems.
In addition, it has been suggested that participatory and co-design approaches em-
phasise that older adults would appreciate AI tools that are emotionally authentic,
respectful, and adaptable to their personal narratives [17]. However, as Abadir et al.
[18] noted, have observed, it is important to be mindful of the potential consequences
of overly insincere displays of empathy, which may lead to scepticism or disengage-
ment if they are not perceived as authentic and trustworthy.
These insights point to additional design priorities for advancing emotionally intelli-
gent AI beyond basic VUI features in reminiscence therapy:
• Deep Emotion Sensing: AI should interpret not only spoken words but also
subtle vocal cues, pauses, and speaking style to read users’ emotional states
more accurately.
• Authenticity and Trust: Emotional responses must feel sincere and context-
aware; forced or generic sympathy can harm trust if it does not fit the situation.
• Guided Emotional Support: AI should not just react but gently guide reflection
or redirect negative emotions when needed, helping users cope with memories in
a safe way.
• Transparent and Ethical Data Use: Handling sensitive affective data must be
clear and respectful, giving users control over how their emotions and stories are
used.
Based on these insights, the next section reviews existing works that support reminis-
cence therapy and identifies the specific research opportunity in this study.
2.4 Existing Works
A variety of digital systems have been developed to support reminiscence therapy (RT)
among older adults, utilising modalities such as multimedia platforms, social apps,
conversational agents, and embodied AI. Each one has its own unique features and
contribution:
Multimedia Systems: Platforms such as the web-based tool described by Wang et al.
[19] enable family members to upload photos and music to facilitate personalised rem-
iniscence sessions. While these tools help stimulate memories, they rely heavily on
caregiver facilitation and typically lack conversational flexibility and dynamic adap-
tation. Similarly, projects like Mnemosyne [20] demonstrate how pervasive displays
and browser-based apps can support reminiscence in care settings but remain largely
rule-based with scripted prompts that do not adapt in real-time to users’ emotions.
Social Platforms: Systems like “Collegamenti” [21] promote intergenerational story-
telling and collaborative memory sharing. Although effective for fostering social en-
7
--- Page 14 ---
gagement, they are generally designed for group contexts and do not address the
nuances of emotional support in one-on-one, home-based sessions.
Conversational Agents: Voice user interfaces and chatbot-based RT tools, such as
the “Know Me Inside-Out” system [22] and other smart speaker applications [23, 24],
lower barriers to participation but often rely on static scripts with limited emotional
responsiveness or adaptation to individual users’ affective states.
Generative AI and Embodied Agents: Recent initiatives like RetroMind [25] and
music-based generative AI systems [26] illustrate the potential for combining large
language models and humanoid robots to deliver personalised, emotionally resonant
dialogue. However, these approaches are still in early stages of research, and their fea-
sibility for everyday home use and integration of real-time emotional feedback remains
limited.
Emotion-Aware Feedback Tools: Some RT research has explored the use of emotion
recognition through EEG signals [27] or post-session affect scoring [28] to monitor
users’ emotional states. But these approaches often rely on intrusive hardware or
retrospective ratings rather than offering lightweight, real-time adaptive feedback for
the user or caregiver during the session.
Overall, these systems demonstrate valuable innovations but share common limita-
tions: dependence on human facilitation, limited adaptivity, and insufficient emotional
sensitivity. This research gap defines the motivation for the present thesis, which aims
to design and evaluate a more emotionally intelligent, voice-based RT system for home
use. Finally, the last section of this chapter synthesises these findings and highlights
the remaining research gaps that this thesis addresses.
2.5 Research Gap
Despite the growing interest in digital systems for reminiscence support, significant
design gaps remain. Existing systems frequently lack the ability to:
• Operate autonomously in home environments without requiring constant care-
giver facilitation.
• Detect and respond to real-time emotional cues during reminiscence sessions.
• Provide caregivers with actionable, emotionally grounded insights from each ses-
sion.
Furthermore, although voice user interfaces (VUIs) are becoming increasingly popular
among older adults due to their accessibility and ease of use, most of them are still
mainly task-oriented and lack emotional intelligence. Systems that cannot adapt to
people’s emotions might make people feel disconnected or upset, especially in emo-
tionally sensitive contexts like reminiscence therapy.
Meanwhile, emotionally intelligent AI has advanced in other areas such as social
robotics and elder companionship, but remains underexplored in the design of digital
RT tools. This disconnect presents a clear opportunity: to develop a lightweight, voice-
based, emotion-aware system that enhances older adults’ engagement while equipping
caregivers with practical tools for therapy that can support and connect with people.

powered, emotionally intelligent interventions that promote aging-in-place and sup-

3 Methodology
This chapter describes the research methodology employed throughout this thesis. A
structured User-Centered Design (UCD) approach guided the entire research process.
The following sections introduce how each phase of the study was carried out.
3.1 Overall Framework
The User-Centered Design (UCD) approach, following the ISO 9241-210 standard [29],
was chosen due to its suitability for addressing users’ specific needs, preferences,
and contexts. This approach emphasizes iterative design, user focus, and ongoing
feedback to ensure usability and accessibility. The overall UCD process adopted in
this project is illustrated in Figure 3.1, which is adapted from ISO 9241-210 [29].
Figure 3.1: User-Centered Design Process (adapted from ISO 9241-210 [29])
The project’s UCD process was structured into four main parts:
1. Context-of-Use Analysis: Contextual inquiry was performed through an exten-
sive literature review and task scenario analysis, synthesizing validated data to
define user characteristics, emotional needs, and environmental constraints for
home-based reminiscence therapy.
2. User Requirements Specification: Insights from the contextual analysis were
translated into functional, emotional, and design requirements that shaped the
system’s goals and key features for developing prototype.
10
--- Page 17 ---
3. Prototype Development: A low-fidelity prototype was created to explore ma-
jor user interface elements, initial user flows and interaction logic. Early-stage
feedbacks were collected through expert reviews, driving the development of a
high-fidelity web prototype with real-time voice interaction and emotion detec-
tion.
4. Usability Evaluation: The high-fidelity prototype was tested remotely with older
adults and informal caregivers. Post-Task Questionnaire, Standard System Us-
ability Questionnaire and a short version of User Experience Questionnaire, were
collected to assess usability and overall user experience.
This UCD process relied heavily on existing knowledge as its foundation, given the
sensitivity of the target users and the constraints of the project timeline. To ground the
design in evidence-based insights, the next section explains how secondary research
was conducted to gather, filter, and synthesise relevant information.
3.2 Secondary Research
Secondary research was conducted to establish the theoretical foundation, define user
and system requirements, and identify relevant gaps to be addressed by the emotion-
ally intelligent voice-based reminiscence system. While Chapter 2 presents the con-
solidated state-of-the-art findings, this section details the systematic process used to
identify, select, analyse, and synthesise existing knowledge for this project.
Given the scope and timeline constraints of the thesis, and the ethical and practical
considerations related to recruiting older adults for new large-scale primary fieldwork,
the project relied on secondary data. Plus reminiscence therapy is a well-established
research domain supported by recent empirical studies, making it appropriate to draw
on validated findings to guide the design and evaluation phases.
3.2.1 Search Strategy
Relevant literature was identified through targeted searches in leading academic databases.
Papers on voice user interfaces, AI, emotion intelligent technology and reminiscence
therapy were sourced primarily from the ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, and Google
Scholar, covering the period from January 2011 to March 2025 to capture both foun-
dational and recent advances. For health-related areas such as psychology and age-
ing research, additional papers were identified through PubMed, ensuring coverage of
clinically relevant interventions in reminiscence therapy and emotional well-being.
The main search strategy combined keywords related to voice interaction, ageing, AI,
emotional AI, and reminiscence therapy. The search string was:
“(voice assistant OR VUI OR AI OR emotional AI) AND (elderly OR older
adults) AND (reminiscence therapy)”
The combined search initially produced a total of 218 records.

3.2.3 Data Extraction and Analysis
Key details from each study, including research aims, context, participant character-

3.3 Context of Use Analysis
The context-of-use analysis translated synthesised evidence into concrete design as-
sets. This phase defined who would use the system, under what conditions, and
which tasks and emotional contexts should be addressed to ensure effective home-
based reminiscence support.
The approach relied primarily on validated findings from prior studies. For example,
Alarcão et al. [28] examined caregivers’ perspectives and environmental challenges in
dementia-focused reminiscence settings. In addition, Lazar et al. [30] systematically
reviewed how information and communication technologies (ICT) can support remi-
niscence therapy for people with dementia, highlighting typical user needs, interaction
barriers, and design considerations such as personalisation, accessibility, and remote
delivery. While the current target group includes older adults more broadly, insights
into emotional needs, practical barriers, and caregiver roles are equally applicable to
non-clinical, home-based contexts.
The analysis combined the following techniques:
• Affinity Diagramming: Key user needs, emotions, and situational factors were
clustered thematically to surface recurring patterns.
• Persona Development: Personas were drafted to represent older adults and
informal caregivers, capturing demographics, motivations, emotional barriers,
and digital literacy traits.
• Task Scenarios: Narrative scenarios were developed for each persona, illustrat-
ing step-by-step how they would engage with the system at home.
• Environmental Mapping: Typical physical and social settings, such as family
living rooms and private bedrooms, were described to highlight constraints and
supportive factors.
These deliverables formed the foundation for defining functional requirements and for
mapping out the user flows in the prototyping stage described next.

3.4 Prototyping Approach
The prototyping process translated conceptual user flows into interaction designs. It
followed an iterative, two-phase structure to minimise design risks and test feasibility
before investing in more complex features.
3.4.1 Low-Fidelity Prototyping
The low-fidelity prototype served to visualise essential user flows and test initial so-
lution. Based on the personas, tasks, and scenarios, annotated wireframes and con-
versation flow diagrams were created. Key focus areas included the caregiver’s profile
setup, the older adult’s voice-based session, and the caregiver’s post-session insight
review.
Expert feedback was gathered through a remote review involving three domain experts:
a UX designer specialised in digital health, an HCI researcher in ageing and accessi-
bility, and a conversational AI developer. They assessed logic, structure, clarity, and
emotional cues. Written feedback was synthesised to identify recurring suggestions,
which informed refinements before the high-fidelity build.
3.4.2 High-Fidelity Prototyping
The high-fidelity prototype made the refined flows with realistic interaction and emo-
tion adaptivity. A web-based format was selected for three reasons:
• Remote Feasibility: Browser delivery enables easy access on common house-
hold devices without installation, lowering barriers for older adults.
• Ecological Validity: Simulating real usage at home supports more authentic
feedback.
• Accessibility: Web platforms have the capacity to comply with WCAG 2.2 guide-
lines, thereby addressing accessibility evaluation.
Key Technical Elements: The prototype integrated the Hume.AI Empathic Voice In-
terface (EVI)[31] to deliver real-time emotion detection during speech, allowing the
system to adapt prompts and feedback dynamically. Next.js structured the front-end
logic for efficient routing and lightweight back-end logic. Radix UI components and
Tailwind CSS to ensure accessible, consistent styling. Session data was stored lo-
cally via IndexedDB and LocalStorage to respect privacy and user control for testing
purpose.
With these technologies, the prototype embodied the core research objective: enabling
emotion-aware, voice-based reminiscence support in everyday home contexts. This
working system then served as the basis for the final evaluation described below.

3.5 Usability Evaluation
The aim of the usability evaluation was to examine whether the high-fidelity prototype
meet its intended goals of supporting home-based reminiscence therapy through an
emotionally intelligent, voice-based system. Specifically, this phase focused on verify-
ing if older adults and their caregivers could successfully complete tasks, experience
emotional engagement, and find the system both usable and meaningful. To guide
this, four main evaluation dimensions were defined:
1. Effectiveness: Whether users could complete key tasks, such as starting a rem-
iniscence session, interacting with the voice assistant, and reviewing emotional
insights.
2. Efficiency: How easily and quickly these tasks could be performed, considering
older adults’ typical cognitive and motor abilities.
3. User Satisfaction and Experience: Users’ subjective perceptions of the system’s
usability and overall design quality, measured using the System Usability Scale
(SUS) and the User Experience Questionnaire (UEQ).
4. Emotional Engagement and Adaptivity: Whether the system’s emotion-aware
interaction was perceived as supportive, and whether caregivers found the post-
session summaries useful and accurate.
The evaluation used a task-based remote usability test designed to simulate realistic
conditions in a home environment. It focused on two core user flows: (1) the experi-
ence of an older adult participating in a guided, emotion-aware reminiscence session,
and (2) the caregiver’s review of the generated emotional summary. These two tasks
reflect the system’s primary goal of combining interactional and emotional value with
practical support from caregivers.

4.1.1 Older Adults
The primary users of the system are older adults aged 65 to 80. This group includes
both cognitively healthy individuals and those diagnosed with mild stages of demen-
tia. Many experience challenges such as memory loss, reduced attention span, or
emotional sensitivity, which can make traditional graphical interfaces or structured
therapy settings difficult to navigate.
Despite these limitations, older adults are capable of engaging meaningfully in remi-
niscence therapy when provided with appropriate support. Voice interaction is partic-
ularly suitable in this context due to its low learning curve and alignment with nat-
ural communication habits developed. By enabling users to share memories through
spoken dialogue, the system seeks to reduce technological barriers and encourage
emotional expression in a safe, familiar format.
4.1.2 Informal Caregivers
Informal caregivers include non-professional individuals such as family members,
adult children, or live-in helpers who support older adults in daily activities. Most
are not trained therapists, though some may have prior caregiving experience. Their
digital literacy is assumed to be moderate to high, particularly among younger family
members. This group interacts with the system indirectly, by setting it up, review-
ing session data, and adjusting prompts. They are central in shaping the therapy
environment and often take emotional responsibility for the older adult’s experience.
4.1.3 Health Professionals
Although not primary users of the system, health professionals such as therapists,
psychologists, or clinical caregivers may benefit from the emotional summaries or ses-
sion data generated by the system. These professionals can provide valuable feedback
or therapeutic guidance to informal caregivers, particularly in cases involving users
with cognitive decline. Their involvement is indirect and consultative, and the system
does not currently provide a dedicated interface for this group. However, designing
with the potential for professional oversight in mind supports long-term integration
into broader care networks.

6 High-Fidelity Prototype
This chapter presents the development of the high-fidelity prototype, which expands
the validated low-fidelity design from Chapter 5 into an interactive, web-based imple-
mentation. This prototype integrates finalised user interface components, live voice

6.1 System Architecture
The system was developed as a client-side web application that delivers real-time,
emotion-aware reminiscence therapy using voice interaction. The architecture follows
a four-layer structure: user interface, dialogue management, local data storage, and
external AI services. A key feature is the integration of Hume.AI’s Empathic Voice
Interface (EVI) for real-time speech recognition, natural language understanding, and
prosody-based emotion detection. Local storage via IndexedDB and localStorage man-
ages user data securely within the user’s browser.

The system architecture comprises four core components: user interface, dialog man-

User Interface Components
The user interface is composed of five main pages. The homepage provides access
to session overviews and navigation to other features. The profiles page allows the
creation and selection of simulated participant profiles, which are used to personalise
session behaviour. The setup page enables users to choose from a curated set of stock
photos, which serve as memory prompts for the reminiscence session. The session
page is the primary interface for voice-based interaction, integrating speech input,
assistant output, and real-time emotional feedback. Finally, the session summary
page presents caregivers with post-session analytics, including emotional trends and
conversation transcripts.
Dialog Manager
At the core of the system is the Dialog Manager, which controls the flow of the conver-
sation. It handles prompt generation, contextual updates, photo switching based on
emotional cues and manages communication with the EVI service. The Dialog Man-
ager ensures that the session remains coherent and emotionally attuned, adjusting
system behaviour dynamically in response to detected user states.
Data Layer
All user data is stored locally in the browser. Structured data such as user profiles and
session logs are saved using IndexedDB, while lightweight items like selected photo IDs
and user preferences are managed with localStorage. This local data strategy minimise
personal information is sent to external servers, ensuring privacy during testing.
The system includes a built-in stock photo library sourced from Pexels [32]. Images
are carefully selected to cover common reminiscence therapy themes and to evoke
emotional resonance whenever possible. Each image is pre-annotated with visual de-
scriptions, emotion tags, and suggested conversation starters generated by the OpenAI
Images Vision API [33] and then reviewed by the researcher. These annotations are
used as input prompts for the voice assistant to guide meaningful reminiscence ses-
sions.
Hume.AI EVI Integration
The system integrates Hume.AI’s Empathic Voice Interface(EVI) which provides real-
time speech-to-text transcription and emotion detection. Hume.AI’s Speech Prosody
model analyses vocal features to produce 48 interpretable dimensions of emotional
meaning, reflecting how people typically perceive emotions through tone. These di-
mensions are not direct indicators of emotional experience but are proxies based on
behavioural patterns[31].

8 Conclusion
This chapter sums up the main findings and explains what they mean for the research
goals and for the wider field of human-computer interaction (HCI) and technology
design for ageing. While earlier chapters described the methodology and results of the
project, this final chapter reflects on the results for conclusions and discussions.
8.1 Conclusion and Discussion
This thesis set out to address the lack of emotional intelligence in tools supporting
home-based reminiscence therapy (RT) for older adults. As outlined in Chapter 1, an
ageing population faces growing challenges like loneliness and cognitive decline. While
RT is well-established, its home use often lacks emotional guidance, personalisation,
and ways for caregivers to track impact. This research aimed to close these gaps by
designing and evaluating a voice-based, emotion-aware system that helps older adults
stay engaged and gives caregivers actionable emotional feedback.
Using a user-centred design approach, a high-fidelity web prototype was built with
Hume.AI’s Empathic Voice Interface, which detects vocal emotional cues in real time,
adapts dialogue flow accordingly for older adult and provide a session summary with
visualization of emotional insights. Usability testing with older adults and caregivers
showed high usability, strong emotional engagement, and promising emotion-adaptive
responses. These results confirm that voice-based, emotionally aware interaction can
enrich home-based RT and support informal caregivers.
8.2 Implications for Design
This study offers practical implications for designing emotionally intelligent, voice-
based systems for older adults and their caregivers. Emotion-aware interactions
should combine real-time sensing with clear, adaptive dialogue to maintain trust and
avoid confusion. Simple, friendly voice tone, natural pacing, and clear cues for turn-
taking can help older adults feel safe when recalling sensitive memories. Personalisa-
tion should go beyond static prompts by allowing the secure use of personal photos or
stories to make reminiscence more meaningful while respecting privacy. In addition,
designers should ensure that emotional feedback is not presented as raw scores alone
but is clear, interpretable, and actionable for caregivers through simple explanations.
More broadly, these insights underline that designing emotion-sensitive AI tools calls
for more than technical accuracy. Transparency, user control, and ethical safeguards
must be built in from the start so that older adults and caregivers know how emo-
tional data is used and can trust the system. By combining affective computing with
thoughtful, human-centred design, future tools can help support ageing-in-place in
ways that are not only practical but also genuinely caring and respectful.

8.3 Contributions and Limitations
This study contributes to HCI specially on empathic design and e-health for healthy
ageing by showing how real-time emotion detection and adaptive voice interaction
can be integrated into everyday reminiscence practice. It proposes a framework that
moves beyond static prompts by linking voice interaction with dynamic emotion-aware
interaction and caregiver-facing insights. Methodologically, the project shows how the
user-centered design process can guide AI development for emotionally sensitive con-
texts. Practically, the system demonstrates that lightweight tools can lower barriers
for older adults to engage in reflection, while helping caregivers plan more supportive
sessions.
However, there are some limits to what this study can show. First, the test group
was small because there were limited resources and time. Only three older adults
and four caregivers took part in the testing, and there was a lack of first-hand field
study beforehand, so the results cannot be used to represent a wider group. Also,
the testing was short-term, so we do not know how well the system would work over
months or years, or what lasting effects it might have. Using stock photos made it
harder to access very personal memories, which can be important in therapy. Finally,
the system only used voice and text to detect emotion. It did not use any of the more
advanced techniques, like facial expressions, which could have made it more accurate.
8.4 Future Direction
Future work could expand personalisation by securely adding user-supplied photos
and exploring hybrid emotion sensing that combines voice with other non-invasive
signals. Studies over longer periods could assess the effects on loneliness, cogni-
tion, and caregiver stress. Further, co-designing with therapists could refine emotion-
adaptation rules for more clinical contexts. Insights from this research can also help
guide designers, developers, and policymakers to make emotion-sensitive AI more
trustworthy and accessible in ageing-in-place scenarios.
In closing, this thesis shows that emotionally intelligent voice systems can make home-
based reminiscence therapy more engaging and supportive. By centring design on
both older adults’ comfort and caregivers’ needs, it offers a path for future AI tools
that support mental well-being at home in more caring, human ways.

A.5 Post-Task Questionnaire (Informal Caregivers)
After reviewing the session dashboard, informal caregiver participants completed a

Closed-ended questions:
1. How easy was it to understand the dashboard? Response options: Very easy,

2. How quickly did you understand the main results?Response options: Very quickly
