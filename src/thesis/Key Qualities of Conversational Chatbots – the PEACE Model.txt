     <div class="abstract"> 
      <p><small> Open-domain chatbots engage in natural conversations with the user to socialize and establish bonds. However, designing and developing an effective open-domain chatbot is challenging. It is unclear what qualities of such chatbots most correspond to users’ expectations. Even though existing work has considered a wide range of aspects, some key components are still missing. More importantly, the consistency and validity of the combined criteria have not been tested. In this paper, we describe a large-scale survey using a consolidated model to elicit users’ preferences, expectations, and concerns. We apply structural equation modeling methods to further validate the data collected from the user survey. The outcome supports the consistency, validity, and reliability of the model, which we call <strong>PEACE</strong> (<strong>P</strong>oliteness, <strong>E</strong>ntertainment, <strong>A</strong>ttentive <strong>C</strong>uriosity, and <strong>E</strong>mpathy). PEACE, therefore, defines the key determinants most predictive of user acceptance. This has allowed us to develop a set of implications useful for the development of compelling open-domain chatbots.</small></p>

      <div class="title-info"> 
       <h2> <span class="section-number">1</span> INTRODUCTION</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <p>Recent advances in Natural Language Processing have revolutionized the Human-Computer Interaction field by turning chatbots into a popular user interface to many services and applications [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0013">13</a>]. Chatbots, also referred to as conversational agents [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>], are Artificial Intelligence (AI) empowered computer programs that interact with the user via natural language, either spoken or written. Scholars broadly classify chatbots into two groups: task-oriented chatbots, which help users reach a specific goal, and open-domain chatbots, which can engage in social chitchat without focusing on any specific subject [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0016">16</a>]. Agents of the former type are extensively introduced by various businesses to provide immediate support to their customers and are also known as intelligent personal assistants [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0018">18</a>]. Agents of the latter type, open-domain chatbots, are meant to become a virtual companion to their users by engaging in small talk with them to socialize and establish bonds; this type of chatbots is yet minimal on the market [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0018">18</a>].</p> 
     <p>With the fast spread of chatbots, numerous studies on user experience with them emerged. As most of the existing chatbots are task-oriented, many research works focused on the functional properties of such agents. Their authors came up with pragmatic recommendations considering such topics as chatbot's ability to reveal its capabilities [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0022">22</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0033">33</a>], consistency in input modalities [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0010">10</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>], and transparency in the privacy policies [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0010">10</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0021">21</a>]. More recently, researchers began examining user expectations about the social qualities of chatbots. Several studies of task-oriented chatbots demonstrated that users try to engage with social chitchat with the agent despite its primarily utilitarian purpose (Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab1">1</a>). Such interactions occur in abundance as the human role metaphor of a chatbot naturally triggers social behavior from the users [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>]. Drawing on these observations, many researchers maintain that future chatbots should be developed following the hybrid framework combining both task-oriented and social agents’ properties [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0065">65</a>].</p>

     <p>A series of qualitative studies specifically analyzed chatbots’ desired social properties, such as personality [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>] and emotion regulation strategies [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0023">23</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0055">55</a>]. However, their focus is fragmented and does not explain how various social traits should integrate into a chatbot. In this perspective, the need arises for a consolidated understanding of what social qualities are most crucial for chatbots to ensure enhanced user experience, leading to technology adoption.</p> 
     <p>Our present work aims to develop a unified model of social qualities for a conversational chatbot based on existing user-centered research on this subject and validate the model using psychometric techniques [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0039">39</a>]. Our purpose is to establish with which social norms users desire their chatbots to comply and how such skills would relate to user intention to engage with the agent. Since the failure of the social component in the existent task-oriented agents causes considerable bottlenecks in spreading chatbot technology, formulating the norms of social etiquette for chatbots has become imperative to shape the efforts of designers and developers of future conversational agents.</p> 
     <p>The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-3">2</a> describes the related work on user expectations and adoption intention of chatbots. Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-6">3</a> details the development of the conceptualized model by defining the constructs and hypothesized relations between them. Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-19">4</a> presents the refinement and evaluation process of the model, including the details about the experiment setup, data analysis, and questionnaire items. Finally, we summarize design implications and future work in Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-26">5</a> and conclude the paper in Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-34">6</a>.</p> 
    </section> 
    <section id="sec-3"> 
     <header> 
      <div class="title-info"> 
       <h2> <span class="section-number">2</span> RELATED WORK</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <section id="sec-4"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">2.1</span> Qualitative and Quantitative User Experience Studies</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>Only few studies of user interaction with open-domain chatbots exist due to their scarcity on the market [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0018">18</a>]. Authors in [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0037">37</a>] explored in a diary study how human-like qualities of the Replika chatbot influence user engagement.<a class="fn" href="#fn1" id="foot-fn1"><sup>1</sup></a> In a different work [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>], authors ran a Wizard-of-Oz (WoZ) experiment to determine the appropriate personality traits for a chatbot. Two other studies [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0024">24</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0055">55</a>] used the qualitative semi-structured interview method to understand how conversational agents can help users treat their emotions.</p> 
      <p>In parallel with these studies, a growing body of work investigates the social aspects of user interaction with task-oriented chatbots. Large et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0028">28</a>] established in a WoZ study that drivers’ interactions with a digital driving assistant were fundamentally social in nature. Authors in [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0046">46</a>] ran a case study of Amazon Alexa and showed that users’ greater personification of the device was positively associated with more sociable uses. Several works focused on users’ social conversational principles with a production-scale question-answer chatbot deployed as a part of the HR system [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>]. Despite the agent's functional purpose, many employees searched for a playful experience with it [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>]. Moreover, users’ social-agent orientation influenced their desire for enhanced conversational abilities of the agent [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>]. Finally, some researchers started to evaluate user experience with the prototypes of hybrid chatbots. Xiao et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>] demonstrated that an interview chatbot with active listening skills is more effective than the one without them. Katayama et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0023">23</a>], in a mixed-method study about the emotionally aware question-answer chatbot, determined that users prefer the agent to adapt its emotionality based on the context.</p> 
      <p>These studies discussed a number of social skills that users expect from their chatbots. However, each study considered only a subset of these qualities at a time and did not relate user expectations to the likelihood of adoption.</p> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-5"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">2.2</span> Psychometric Studies of Chatbot Adoption</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>Several recent research works have applied psychometric methods to study factors influencing user adoption. Some of them also included the social and affective qualities of chatbots. Authors in [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0069">69</a>] employed the Consumer Acceptance of Technology (CAT) model [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0025">25</a>] to test what determinants influence user intention to use a movie ticket booking chatbot. Their model included determinants from the cognitive and affective dimensions. While the cognitive items were measured with multi-item instruments, the authors employed the visual Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM) scale [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0004">4</a>] to measure the affective features. Determinants from both dimensions had a significant impact on the chatbot's effectiveness. However, a coarse assessment of the affective dimension cannot be easily translated into actionable design recommendations. In a different work [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0036">36</a>], the authors applied the extended Unified Theory of Adoption and Use of Technology (UTAUT2) model [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0058">58</a>] to analyze user intentions to use a chatbot to organize vacation trips. The model consisted of 11 constructs in total, with only two describing the chatbot's social abilities: communicating in a human-like manner and entertaining the user. Both were found to impact the adoption positively, but they fail to cover the whole range of chatbot's social capabilities. Finally, another study of a customer service chatbot investigated the mediating role of an agent's human-like qualities between its ability to resolve miscommunication and user adoption [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0052">52</a>]. Anthropomorphic chatbots that seek clarification in case of misunderstanding were found to satisfy customers’ social desires and be as effective as idealized error-free agents. While being a promising finding, the authors did not elaborate on which anthropomorphic qualities are specifically vital for chatbots.</p> 
      <p>Each of the works above studies a dedicated domain-specific chatbot and considers its human-like qualities only partially. They fail to provide an integrated view on how different anthropomorphic skills of a chatbot predict user engagement. To facilitate the developmental efforts and bring conceptual clarity, our study aims to establish a unified model of social qualities for chatbots and assess their impact on user intention to accept this technology. We specifically choose to focus exclusively on the characteristics typically attributed to open-domain chatbots: once systematized, they can be easily introduced for any domain-specific application within the framework of hybrid agents.</p> 
     </section> 
    </section> 
    <section id="sec-6"> 
     <header> 
      <div class="title-info"> 
       <h2> <span class="section-number">3</span> MODEL DEVELOPMENT</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <p>Our goal is to understand what social qualities laypeople expect from future chatbots and which of them predict user intention to adopt them. The initial step involves identifying the component constructs of the model and generating sample questions representing the concepts under consideration. To achieve this goal, we draw inspiration from the Expectation-Confirmation Theory (ECT) [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0003">3</a>]. It postulates that before the purchase or adoption, users form a set of initial expectations about a specific product or service, which further influence their initial decision to adopt the given item. In turn, according to Ratchford and Barnhart, these expectations may be influenced by the users’ individual characteristics and attitudes [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0048">48</a>]. Therefore, we decided to structure the question items into three high-level layers of constructs: <em>user attitudes</em>, <em>expected chatbot qualities</em>, and <em>behavioral intentions</em>.</p> 
     <p>We then carefully surveyed prior work to identify essential user experience criteria to form the principal constructs. The consecutive work involves several iterations of phrasing the participating questions until their semantic meanings are clear to the users. Below, we explain the constructs in more detail by reviewing the existing works that have inspired us to derive them and describe the hypotheses relating the constructs.</p> 
     <section id="sec-7"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">3.1</span> User Attitudes</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>The first layer of the model, <em>user attitudes</em>, describes the extent to which a person has a favorable or unfavorable appraisal of given objects and actions [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0001">1</a>]. Depending on the individual attitudes, people may form distinctive expectations about their interaction with new technology [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0048">48</a>]. Factors constituting this dimension are exogenous variables of the model; thus, we needed evidence from prior work that they indeed influence user expectations and behaviors. Based on the thorough literature review, we focus on three principal dimensions: user openness to technologies, vulnerability, and empathy propensity.</p> 
      <section id="sec-8"> 
       <p><em>3.1.1 Openness to Technologies.</em> Openness to technologies measures user's personal disposition towards new IT products and services. In a study evaluating users’ expectations and acceptance of futuristic Augmented Reality (AR) scenarios, Olsson et al. demonstrated that evaluation results might be considerably affected by the respondents’ general orientation and attitude towards novel technologies [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0040">40</a>]. We adapted the items from the Technology Adoption Propensity (TAP) index [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0048">48</a>] to account for this factor. Specifically, we combined the questions from the “optimism” and “proficiency” sub-scales, which are identified as two attitudinal factors contributing towards the technology adoption. The “optimism” sub-scale assesses users’ belief that new technologies provide more flexibility in their everyday lives, and the “proficiency” sub-scale evaluates their sense of being technologically competent. We decided to merge the items from these two sub-scales under a single construct to save the respondents’ cognitive load.</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-9"> 
       <p><em>3.1.2 Vulnerability.</em> Vulnerability is another construct of the TAP index, which refers to users’ belief that technology increases their chances of being taken advantage of by criminals or firms [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0048">48</a>]. Contrary to the previously discussed constructs, vulnerability acts as an inhibitor of adoption propensity; users who have significant concerns about their security and privacy are typically reluctant to use new technologies for fear of being victimized. Previous qualitative studies of user experience with chatbots defined two principal factors affecting users’ sense of vulnerability: <strong>monetary harm</strong> and <strong>social harm</strong>. Monetary harm refers to the potential damage of the misuse of a user's financial data, e.g., her banking information being sold to third parties [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0010">10</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0021">21</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Social harm is associated with disclosing sensitive personal information, such as announcing a significant life event, which might lead to social embarrassment [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0010">10</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0021">21</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>].</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-10"> 
       <p><em>3.1.3 Empathy Propensity.</em> Empathy propensity describes a person's disposition to understand and respond adaptively to others’ emotions. Prior research in affective neuroscience found that people establishing empathetic attitudes towards others tend to prefer closer interpersonal distance during social interaction [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0042">42</a>]. As users typically apply the norms and principles of social interaction to technology [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0049">49</a>], their expectations of chatbots’ social qualities should be influenced by this factor. Thus far, Tsiourti et al. employed empathy propensity assessment to evaluate user perception of the robot's emotion and concluded that users with a higher disposition of empathy could manage this task more accurately [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0056">56</a>]. In a study of user requirements for chatbot design, Liao et al. demonstrated that users with higher social-agent orientation prefer their chatbots to handle conversations in a more human-like manner, providing subjective and opinionated answers and presenting relational behaviors [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>]. For our study, we decided to exploit the Toronto Empathy Questionnaire [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0053">53</a>] to assess users’ empathy propensity and explore its influence on their expectations.</p> 
      </section> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-11"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">3.2</span> Expected Chatbot Qualities</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>The second layer in the model, <em>expected chatbot qualities</em>, refers to different social and emotional qualities that users might expect from the chatbots. At this layer, we combined a wide range of qualities commonly discussed in the related work on user experience with chatbots as well as papers focusing on the technical implementation of these skills.</p> 
      <section id="sec-12"> 
       <p><em>3.2.1 Politeness.</em> The politeness construct captures users’ desire for chatbots to demonstrate respectful and considerate behavior in several ways. Firstly, it reflects the expectation for chatbots to respond decently and avoid rude language, as agents failing to comply with these norms cause embarrassment and frustration [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0014">14</a>]. Additionally, the results of prior qualitative studies indicated that the idea of a chatbot that can listen to human interlocutors without judging them was appealing to the users [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0005">5</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. As people may sometimes find it demanding to adhere to politeness norms around-the-clock regardless of the situation, this quality of chatbots creates a unique value proposition for their users [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Well-mannered, not argumentative agents are more likely to inspire trust and ensure user engagement [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>].</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-13"> 
       <p><em>3.2.2 Entertainment.</em> Entertainment is an essential aspect of chatbots. Many users first decide to engage with these agents due to the promise of playful interactions [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0033">33</a>]. As a construct in our model, it comprises several interconnected social skills that users seek in chatbots. One of them is generating compelling and diverse responses. Diversity in chatbot's responses is a challenging task that has been actively studied from the developmental perspective [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0029">29</a>]. From the users’ point of view, it could drive engagement in conversations with the chatbot, help avoid repetitions, and promote fun [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0022">22</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0051">51</a>]. Another essential element of entertainment is a chatbot's ability to demonstrate its sense of humor by generating amusing responses, puns, and witty one-liners [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0022">22</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0033">33</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0066">66</a>].</p> 
       <p>Along with it, merely establishing social contact with the user is also an important quality associated with the adoption of conversational agents [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>]. Previous qualitative works considered introductory phrases, small talk, and social conversations as a type of playful interaction with the chatbot frequently sought by the users (see also Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab1">1</a>) [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0005">5</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0022">22</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0062">62</a>]. Finally, we also included the chatbot's ability to include emojis and interjections into its responses as a part of the entertainment construct. Prior research has shown that the inclusion of such cognitive-emotional expression improves user experience and acts as “socio-affective glue” in developing rapport between the user and the agent [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0009">9</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0012">12</a>].</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-14"> 
       <p><em>3.2.3 Attentive Curiosity.</em> Attentive curiosity measures users’ expectations about the chatbot's active listening, anticipation, and adaptability skills. Active listening is the ability to understand and respond to the user appropriately [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>]. Chatbots with active listening skills could elicit quality user responses [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>] and appeared as more appealing and engaging [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0051">51</a>]. One technique for chatbots to practice active listening is to ask impromptu questions based on user response, i.e., demonstrate curiosity [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>]. Combined with the ability to “remember” previous user replies, chatbots can show attention to the users by anticipating their needs and bringing up exciting topics – a behavior that the users would highly appreciate [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Moreover, question-asking could help chatbots avoid misunderstanding with the users [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0052">52</a>] and reason about their preferred social distance to adjust the conversational style accordingly [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0037">37</a>].</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-15"> 
       <p><em>3.2.4 Empathy.</em> Empathy is the chatbot's ability to recognize and respond to user emotion appropriately. Researchers focusing on open-domain chatbot development have been carrying long-lasting efforts to empower these agents with the ability to express emotions in their responses [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0002">2</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0061">61</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0070">70</a>]. In turn, authors exploring people's expectations about chatbots identified that users also desire them to follow specific interaction patterns when treating user emotion, generally establishing reassuring and empathetic behavior [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Further technical papers demonstrated promising results by training neural network-based empathetic chatbots on the datasets of empathetic human dialogs [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0020">20</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0047">47</a>], which confirms the importance of considering this factor in our model.</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-16"> 
       <p><em>3.2.5 Personality.</em> This factor determines whether users expect a chatbot to exhibit a personality. Prior qualitative and WoZ studies reported that most users were willing to have their chatbots endowed with this quality. Jain et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0022">22</a>] found that people preferred agents with distinct personalities that matched their operational domain. In a study of a question-answer chatbot, Liao et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>] concluded that users with high social-agent orientation desired it to present a personality despite the agent's task-oriented nature. In a WoZ experiment, Thies et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>] specifically considered different personality traits for a chatbot to identify which ones would work best for their target audience.</p> 
      </section> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-17"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">3.3</span> Behavioral Intentions</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>Behavioral intentions towards a chatbot are related to whether or not the chatbot can influence users’ decision to use it if it were endowed with the discussed social and emotional skills. According to the Theory of Planned Behavior [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0057">57</a>], behavioral intention can be a strong predictor of actual behavior. Thus, asking the users to evaluate their behavioral intentions concerning such chatbots based on their expectations is a useful and insightful approach, especially when actual chatbots having a full set of the discussed qualities are non-existent. Following the practice of previous adoption-related studies [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0045">45</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0069">69</a>], in the behavioral intentions construct, we assess users’ intention to use the chatbot and their desire to introduce a chatbot to their friends.</p> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-18"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">3.4</span> Hypotheses</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>To evaluate our model, we form a set of hypotheses about how various constructs relate to each other (Figure <a class="fig" href="#fig1">1</a>). We hypothesize that users’ attitudes have an influence on their expectations about chatbots. Specifically, we posit that openness to technologies and empathy propensity would have a positive effect on users’ expectations about chatbot's social skills. At the same time, a sense of vulnerability would influence them negatively. We also hypothesize a significant positive causal effect from users’ expectations to their intention to use socially and emotionally aware chatbots. In the following sections, we present the evaluation of our model and the hypotheses. </p>

       <h2> <span class="section-number">4</span> MODEL EVALUATION</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <section id="sec-20"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.1</span> Experiment Setup</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>To validate the conceptualized model and the hypotheses, we developed a structured online survey. The survey questions focused on users’ level of empathy, openness to technologies in general, expectations and concerns about conversational chatbots, and intention to use them. We used a validated scale to measure users’ empathy propensity level [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0053">53</a>] and designed other questions based on the literature review discussed in the previous sections.</p> 
      <p>We thoughtfully selected the surveyed population sample to ensure that our participants were familiar with chatbot technology and could meaningfully reflect their expectations in the responses. To account for possible limitations of the self-report data collection method, we carried a careful validity and reliability evaluation of the resultant model. The details are revealed in the following sections.</p> 
      <p>First, we launched two consecutive pilot surveys (N=50 each). The purpose of the pilot surveys was to verify the process of online data collection, test the understandability of the questions, and validate the payment adequacy based on participants’ feedback. We then finalized the questionnaire based on the response patterns and feedback.</p> 
      <p>The survey consisted of four major sections: introduction and instructions, basic demographic survey, main questions, and the concluding part, where participants could provide their feedback about the survey in an open-ended manner. Demographic questions included age, gender, English fluency, and profession. The central part consisted of 64 Likert-type items covering constructs in our model with response options from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (5). We deliberately included several redundant questions, such as questions of similar meanings or semantically opposite and negated meanings, to check responses for adequacy and consistency and control data quality.</p> 
      <p>We conducted the main large-scale survey on Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT). We set a payment of 41 cents for completing the task. Paolacci et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0041">41</a>] suggested that AMT is a viable platform for data collection if the data quality is controlled. Therefore, we required a 95% HIT approval rate<a class="fn" href="#fn2" id="foot-fn2"><sup>2</sup></a> for the AMT workers and additionally inspected the data for random or irresponsible answers. A bonus of 25 cents was paid to workers who passed the attention check. We also required all the participants to be based in the US for several reasons. We wanted to eliminate the possibility of question misunderstanding caused by language-related issues (our questionnaire was prepared in English). Besides, we considered the US population to be sufficiently homogeneous in terms of the average level of the population's familiarity with the chatbot technology [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0005">5</a>], which was necessary to ensure that the respondents could relate to the questions. The survey was launched on July 6th at 5:30 p.m. (CEST) and completed on July 7th at 3:30 p.m. (CEST) in 2020. In total, 1000 workers completed the survey during the elapsed 22 hours.</p> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-21"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.2</span> Data Description and Participants</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>We describe how we checked the responses for their quality. Firstly, we discarded 25 respondents as they failed to submit a complete survey form. Secondly, we computed the variance of each worker's responses to all questions. We discarded respondents with zero variance (who selected the same score for every question), as they did not have natural variation in their answers; there were 8 such cases in total. Then, we inspected the pairs of reverse scale questions to test inconsistencies in participants’ responses. In total, we included 5 pairs of such questions. If a contradiction in worker's answers was found in more than 3 cases, their submission was discarded. We dropped 231 submissions based on this criterion. Lastly, we detected potential automatic bots by examining each worker's elapsed time to complete the survey. If the elapsed time was less than 5 minutes, the submission was considered invalid as it was unlikely for a human with average reading and clicking speeds to answer every question in 4 seconds. We filtered out 200 such cases. The final sample size of valid users became 536. The rule of thumb is to have at least 10 participants for each question item [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0026">26</a>]; thus, our sample size is sufficient for a stable factor estimate.</p> 
      <p>Among the selected 536 participants, females (51%) and males (49%) were represented approximately equally. More than 60% of participants were in the 31-65 age group, 34% were in the 18-30 age group, and the rest belonged to users above 65. As mentioned earlier, all participants were based in the US, and the vast majority (96%) indicated that they were native English speakers. Their occupations were diverse: 14% were self-employed, 7% were engineers, and the remaining included managers (5%), students (5%), programmers (4%), homemakers (3%), and others. Based on their professions, we roughly split the participants into IT-related and not IT-related, resulting in 17% and 83% correspondingly. The details of all participants are summarized in Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab2">2</a>.</p> 
      <p>One of the survey questions asked the participants to rate their previous experience with a <em>socially and emotionally aware</em> agent and specify this chatbot's name. Most participants scored low on this question (Mean: 1.94, SD: 1.27), validating our hypothesis that such agents are relatively scarce on the market. The prevailing majority could not provide the name of a chatbot endowed with social skills (97%). The most frequently mentioned agent was Alexa (6 mentions). However, the respondents who specified it indicated uncertainty about its social capabilities, as exemplified by the comment: “I use Alexa but I think she needs to be able to show more emotion.”</p>

     <section id="sec-22"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.3</span> Analysis Methods</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>We validated the rationality of each construct of the conceptualized model and their relationships using the techniques described in [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0039">39</a>]. We first validated that a relationship between multiple observed variables and the underlying latent constructs exist by applying Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA). The CFA results are presented in Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-24">4.5</a>. We then conducted path analysis in structural equation modeling (SEM) to examine the causal relationship between the latent variables in the model. The findings of SEM are reported in Section <a class="sec" href="#sec-25">4.6</a>. We used the R-lavaan package version 0.6.6 to conduct the analysis.<a class="fn" href="#fn3" id="foot-fn3"><sup>3</sup></a> We employed the maximum likelihood estimation with robust standard errors and a Satorra-Bentler scaled test statistic (<tt>MLM</tt> estimator) to account for the ordinal nature of the Likert-type data [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0050">50</a>].</p> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-23"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.4</span> Data Screening</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>Before validating the model, we conducted data screening to ensure the dataset is reliable for testing causal relationships [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0008">8</a>]. All data items were complete without any missing values. Second, we needed to ensure that the data for all variables are well modeled by a normal distribution to satisfy SEM assumptions [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. We assessed the empirical distributions of responses for each question visually and eliminated the items that deviated considerably from the bell-curved distribution [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. Further, we checked the skewness and kurtosis of the remaining items. Results show that the skewness (− 1.03, 0.15) and kurtosis (− 1.07, 0.35) of all variables fall into the (− 2.00, 2.00) interval, the recommended acceptable range of normality test for Likert-type questions [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0017">17</a>]. Thus our data also meet normality requirements.</p> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-24"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.5</span> Model Validity and Reliability</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>To validate the model, we first assessed its internal consistency and reliability using Chronbach's alpha and item-to-total correlations. This procedure aims at revealing the internal consistencies of the constructs and identifying the clusters of related variables. The items with a low alpha value (&lt; 0.6) were discarded or re-grouped into another construct. After several iterations, we obtained the values presented in Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab4">4</a>. They meet the cut-off points of 0.6 for Chronbach's alpha [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0043">43</a>] and 0.3 for item-to-total correlation [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0039">39</a>].</p> 
      <p>We examined the convergent validity of the measurement items by composite reliability (CR) and average variance extracted (AVE) based on factor loadings from the Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0015">15</a>]. The results are shown in Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab4">4</a>. Factor loadings for all items exceeded the acceptable level of 0.5 [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. Composite reliability for all constructs also exceeded the recommended level of 0.6 [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0015">15</a>]. For average variance extracted, most of the constructs met the recommended 0.5 level [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0015">15</a>] except for two constructs, Politeness and Attentive Curiosity, which scored slightly below the threshold. However, according to Lam [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0027">27</a>] and Fornell and Larcker [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0015">15</a>], the average variance extracted may be a more conservative estimate of the validity of the measurement model, and “on the basis of <em>p<sub>n</sub></em> (composite reliability) alone, the researcher may conclude that the convergent validity of the construct is adequate, even though more than 50% of the variance is due to error” [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0015">15</a>]. As CR values for the two constructs, Politeness and Attentive Curiosity, are above the recommended cut-off points, we conclude that the convergent validity of the measurement items is acceptable.</p>

     <section id="sec-25"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">4.6</span> Structural Equation Modeling</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>We tested the overall model fit regarding our hypotheses (Figure <a class="fig" href="#fig1">1</a>) on the causal relationships among the three layers of constructs. Figure <a class="fig" href="#fig2">2</a> shows the results of the structural model analysis, including the R2 (coefficients of determination) and path loadings. All the R2 estimates for the variables are larger than the threshold of 0.1; thus, they are appropriate and informative to examine the significance of the associated paths [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0045">45</a>]. The model fit indices are <em>χ</em><sup>2</sup> = 754.273, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001, <em>df</em> = 357, <em>χ</em><sup>2</sup>/<em>df</em> = 2.11 (&lt; 3), <em>CFI</em> = 0.921 (&gt; 0.9), <em>TLI</em> = 0.910 (&gt; 0.9), <em>RMSEA</em> = 0.051 (&lt; 0.08), <em>SRMS</em> = 0.058 (&lt; 0.08), which surpass the recommended values of these model fit indices (shown in brackets) [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. It is desirable for the <em>χ</em><sup>2</sup> statistic to achieve statistically insignificant goodness of fit value, but in practice, it is difficult to obtain with large sample sizes [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. For this reason, it is recommended to refer to the normed <em>χ</em><sup>2</sup>, defined as a ratio of <em>χ</em><sup>2</sup> to the degrees of freedom for the model (<em>χ</em><sup>2</sup>/<em>df</em>), to correct for the bias against large samples and increased model complexity [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0019">19</a>]. </p>
      <figure id="fig2"> 
       <img src="https://dl-acm-org.aalto-libproxy.idm.oclc.org/cms/attachment/html/10.1145/3397481.3450643/assets/html/images/iui21-9-fig2.jpg" class="img-responsive" alt="Structural model fit" aria-describedby="fig003" /> 
       <p style="display:none" id="fig003">The diagram demonstrates the structural model with path loadings and coefficients of determination.</p> 
       <figcaption> <span class="figure-number">Figure 2:</span> <span class="figure-title">Structural model fit. Path significance: ***<em>p</em> &lt; 0.001, **<em>p</em> &lt; 0.05, *<em>p</em> &lt; 0.1.</span> 
       </figcaption> 
      </figure> 
      <p></p> 
      <p>We first examined the path relationships between the constructs included in user attitudes and expected chatbot qualities. Openness to technologies has a significant positive influence on user expectations about chatbot's empathy (<em>β</em> = 0.351, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001), attentive curiosity (<em>β</em> = 0.358, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001), and entertainment (<em>β</em> = 0.467, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001) qualities. Empathy propensity significantly leads to the expected politeness of the chatbot (<em>β</em> = 0.617, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001). Moreover, the sense of vulnerability diminishes user expectations. Specifically, concerns about monetary harm have a significant negative effect on empathy (<em>β</em> = −0.231, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001), attentive curiosity (<em>β</em> = −0.197, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001), and entertainment (<em>β</em> = −0.098, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.1). User worries about social harm also have a slightly negative influence on empathy (<em>β</em> = −0.017, <em>p</em> &gt; 0.1) and attentive curiosity (<em>β</em> = −0.012, <em>p</em> &gt; 0.1), though these paths are insignificant.</p> 
      <p>Furthermore, user expectations about chatbot's politeness significantly affect the ones about chatbot's empathy (<em>β</em> = 0.283, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001) and attentive curiosity (<em>β</em> = 0.545, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001). As for the relationship between expected chatbot qualities and behavioral intentions, we found that expectations about chatbot's empathy (<em>β</em> = 0.296, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.05), attentive curiosity (<em>β</em> = 0.176, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.05), and entertainment (<em>β</em> = 0.418, <em>p</em> &lt; 0.001) most influence the use and share intentions.</p>

    <section id="sec-26"> 
     <header> 
      <div class="title-info"> 
       <h2> <span class="section-number">5</span> DISCUSSION</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <p>The presented evaluation process proved the hypotheses relating the constructs (Figure <a class="fig" href="#fig1">1</a>) and produced the final PEACE model, comprising the essential social and emotional qualities for conversational chatbots: Politeness, Entertainment, Attentive Curiosity, and Empathy. The analysis supported prior research results [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0040">40</a>], suggesting that personal differences in user beliefs and attitudes may significantly influence their expectations about future technology. We established that a generally positive attitude to technologies amplifies users’ desire for conversational agents to display more natural social behavior. Likewise, a higher level of users’ empathy propensity was found to predict their stronger desire for chatbot's politeness, understanding, and non-judging support. At the same time, the results of psychometric evaluation demonstrated that users’ sense of vulnerability might impede their willingness to experience more socially aware technology in the future. The threat of becoming a victim of a financial fraud restrains user expectations, which is understandable as emotional triggers and social influence are typical techniques employed by perpetrators of online scams [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0059">59</a>]. Besides, the risks of social embarrassment in case of sensitive information disclosure also slightly reduce user expectations about chatbot's empathetic and attentive curiosity skills, though this effect is less pronounced.</p> 
     <p>According to the analysis of the PEACE model, special attention should be paid to the politeness factor. While there is no direct path from politeness to behavioral intentions, it has a strong significant influence on the other two expected chatbot qualities, making it a key player in the model. One necessary condition for a user to consider trying out a chatbot is its outstanding value proposition compared to existing services for that individual [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0005">5</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Unconditional politeness and a premise of non-judging interaction are among the main requirement for open-domain chatbots [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0035">35</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. If this condition is satisfied, users may consider engaging further in more social interaction with the agent.</p> 
     <p>Interestingly, the politeness construct has a more substantial influence on attentive curiosity rather than empathy. One reasoning behind this finding could be the following. Empathetic behavior suggests demonstrating compassion and support but not necessarily scrutinizing the details about the user's particular emotional state or experience. Meanwhile, attentive curiosity implies an agent's follow-up questions that could help it learn more context about the user. People typically feel more comfortable sharing personal information when they have a perception of psychological safety to communicate, i.e., the possibility to speak up without risks of being blamed or disapproved [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0064">64</a>]. Thus, politeness appears to be even more critical for scenarios when a chatbot inquires further details and information from the user.</p> 
     <p>The other three social qualities, apart from politeness, have a direct positive influence on behavioral intentions. Unsurprisingly, entertainment shows the most prominent effect. So far, this is probably the only skill that is already widely implemented in existing chatbots, e.g., Google's Easter Eggs.<a class="fn" href="#fn4" id="foot-fn4"><sup>4</sup></a> Possibly drawing on their previous positive experience with playful agents, users demonstrated a notable desire for this quality to be maintained and further developed in the chatbots. On the contrary, attentive curiosity and empathy of chatbots are much less familiar constructs for the users. Only few research works study user perceptions of these qualities in prototypical chatbots so far, e.g., [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0020">20</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0060">60</a>], so the vast majority of the respondents had to rely on their genuine desire to judge these constructs. Nevertheless, we detected a strong user interest for these chatbot qualities as well. Almost all questions comprising empathy and attentive curiosity factors scored higher than 3.5 points out of 5 (Table <a class="tbl" href="#tab4">4</a>), and the constructs themselves established a significant predicting power on user behavioral intentions.</p> 
     <p>The presented PEACE model shows promising prospects for future open-domain and hybrid chatbots endowed with social and emotional intelligence. To inform the community of chatbot developers and designers, we extract the key implications of our study below and outline our vision for future work.</p> 
     <section id="sec-27"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">5.1</span> Implications</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <section id="sec-28"> 
       <p><em>5.1.1 Attitudes strongly influence expectations.</em> The evaluation of the PEACE model demonstrated that individual differences in people's attitudes play a considerable role in forming their expectations about future chatbot technology. As modern applications typically allow service providers to get at least some initial knowledge about the user, it is recommended to use this advantage to tailor user experience with their chatbot and ensure a better match to user expectations. In line with prior findings [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0030">30</a>], we confirmed that users with higher empathy propensity are much more interested in signs of humanness in chatbots than their more pragmatic counterparts. Further, if the user is a tech-savvy person, she is even more likely to appreciate the social qualities of a chatbot. No less important, one should also account for the users’ concerns and effectively communicate the adopted policies for privacy and data security.</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-29"> 
       <p><em>5.1.2 Politeness is key.</em> Politeness proved to be an essential prerequisite for people to consider engagement with socially intelligent conversational agents. Given the popularity of data-driven approaches to developing novel neural network-based chatbots, it is critical for developers to meticulously control the quality of the training data for their conversational models. Otherwise, it is unlikely that the resulting chatbot will last long, as it was well illustrated by the example of the infamous Tay chatbot.<a class="fn" href="#fn5" id="foot-fn5"><sup>5</sup></a> After being released on Twitter to learn from uncontrolled crowd-sourced data, it was terminated in less than 20 hours for its excessively offensive and inflammatory comments [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0038">38</a>]. To avoid such scenarios, developers should opt for carefully prepared high-quality datasets, e.g., as provided in work by Rashkin et al. [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0047">47</a>], or regulate unethical chatbot's replies by other means, e.g., employing active learning techniques [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0032">32</a>].</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-30"> 
       <p><em>5.1.3 Entertainment is king.</em> Entertainment and humor form a common “entry point” to chatbot technology, even for the functional chatbots operating in domain-specific settings [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0033">33</a>]. Our analysis of the open-domain case further supported the significance of this quality, demonstrating its superior leading effect on user adoption intention compared to the three other qualities. Developers should put effort into cultivating a chatbot's sense of humor and reinforcing it with the use of relevant interjections and emojis. Notably, the ability to engage in small talk is also considered a playful aspect of the agent's behavior [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0031">31</a>] and should be provisioned as a part of the chatbot's social intelligence.</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-31"> 
       <p><em>5.1.4 Attentive curiosity is essential for personalization.</em> According to our findings, users would be willing to share their thoughts and information with the agent once the politeness requirement is satisfied. We recommend that developers incorporate active listening and impromptu questioning capabilities into their chatbots to gradually build a user image and use it to accommodate user expectations. Impromptu questions can be generated based on the user's conversational intent disclosed in the input utterance [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0054">54</a>] to deepen the conversation and elicit additional knowledge about the user [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0067">67</a>]. One essential factor shaping the user experience is the chatbot's alignment with the user style and language. Adapting it accordingly may ensure more personalization and improve engagement [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0006">6</a>]. Moreover, being informed about its users’ extended profiles, a conversational agent should be able to anticipate their needs better and propose topics for discussion matching users’ interests with greater precision.</p> 
      </section> 
      <section id="sec-32"> 
       <p><em>5.1.5 Empathy shows support.</em> Qualitative user studies have previously mentioned users’ desire for chatbots to understand their emotional states and provide the necessary emotional support [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0005">5</a>, <a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0068">68</a>]. Our results proved this to be one of the decisive factors influencing users’ desire to accept a chatbot. Thus, it is vital for future agents to combine both the ability to detect the emotion expressed by the user accurately and generate an emotionally appropriate response. Some promising results have already been demonstrated for the customer service chatbot [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0020">20</a>], and we suggest that they be extended further on a more general conversational case.</p> 
      </section> 
     </section> 
     <section id="sec-33"> 
      <header> 
       <div class="title-info"> 
        <h3> <span class="section-number">5.2</span> Limitations and Future Work</h3> 
       </div> 
      </header> 
      <p>Several questions and constructs were eliminated from the model due to low correlations with other variables, such as user expectations about the chatbot's personality. We presume that personality failed to segregate as a standalone construct as the traits defining one's personality (i.e., the Big Five personality traits [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0034">34</a>]) are considerably dispersed across the other four established constructs. Therefore, we still recommend treating personality as an important aspect of a conversational chatbot.<a class="fn" href="#fn6" id="foot-fn6"><sup>6</sup></a> However, to identify its influence on user adoption, more controlled experiments should be performed to show which personality traits are the most influential.</p> 
      <p>We chose to target our study at the US-based population and used a survey to collect the data. Future research should further validate the PEACE model with other populations and analyze cultural influences (e.g., American vs. European vs. Asian users). Employing alternative research methods, such as analyzing user reviews of existing open-domain chatbots [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0046">46</a>], could also be beneficial to offer additional validity assessment for the findings originating from the self-report studies.</p> 
      <p>Our study focused on user expectations as they provide a reference level for users to form evaluative judgments about the system and serve as an additional determinant of satisfaction [<a class="bib" data-trigger="hover" data-toggle="popover" data-placement="top" href="#BibPLXBIB0003">3</a>]. To extend the established results and account for potential changes in user expectations following their interaction experience with the technology, our future technical work includes exploring the ways to develop a chatbot with the discussed social skills and evaluating actual user experience with such an agent. Once a sufficiently stable and functioning prototype of a socially intelligent chatbot is available, a similar study could extend the current work by further evaluating how behavioral intentions influence users’ actual use of the system. For each particular application, it might be necessary to tailor the degree of the chatbot's social and emotional capabilities considering its specific purpose and operational domain.</p> 
     </section> 
    </section> 
    <section id="sec-34"> 
     <header> 
      <div class="title-info"> 
       <h2> <span class="section-number">6</span> CONCLUSION</h2> 
      </div> 
     </header> 
     <p>This paper presented an overview of recent user experience research devoted to conversational chatbots. Careful examination of the results from numerous related studies led to the conceptualization of the PEACE model – the first consolidated model of essential social and emotional qualities of chatbots that predict user desire to accept this technology. The psychometric evaluation procedure confirmed that our model provided the validity and reliability of its constructs and verified that the paths between them established meaningful causal relationships.</p> 
     <p>In our work, we detailed how we combined and validated existing criteria into a unified model. This model defines key determinants motivating the adoption intention of open-domain conversational agents, i.e., the agent's abilities to follow the <strong>P</strong>oliteness protocol, <strong>E</strong>ntertain the user, practice <strong>A</strong>ttentive <strong>C</strong>uriosity towards the user, and express <strong>E</strong>mpathy, thus PEACE. Our results were able to extend beyond the prior work, which focused only on a few assessment criteria at a time and provided a meaningful explanation of the overall user requirements and desiderata for future socially-empowered chatbots. Finally, we summarized the principal insights of the study in a set of design implications informing future efforts of the design and development of emotionally and socially aware conversational agents.</p> 
    </section>
